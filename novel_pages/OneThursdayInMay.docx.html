Chapter 1

For a relatively small store, the Handy Dandy Grocery and Deli did a hopping business every afternoon but Sunday, when the owners, Bob and Margy Lippart, against current norms and all fiscal studies of the time, closed the store for the day.  Therefore, it was no surprise that, on this Thursday, even with swirling storm clouds and a darkness rivaling nighttime descending on the town of Pascal, just outside of Dallas, Texas, the store was crowded with shoppers.

Elliott Paulson looked behind him at the large front windows and groaned.  His shift was almost over, but he wouldn’t get out of here before the storm hit.  And it looked like it was going to be a doozy.  As if to prove his point, a clap of thunder and simultaneous lightning strike, sounding like a close-range sonic boom, inspired a series of startled gasps and screams throughout the store and caused the senior woman in his line to drop her change purse, coins pinging and rolling across the floor.  Elliott closed his eyes briefly and groaned.  She was his last customer.  She was only buying a quart of buttermilk.  Now he was going to have to chase her money across the floor.

She looked up at him apologetically, and Elliott smiled, sighing.  “It’s okay, ma’am.  You wait here,” he said, automatically keying in his log-out code on the register.

“I’m so sorry, young man,” the woman muttered shakily, looking down at the scattered change, “that was just such a –“ She stopped as a new noise invaded the store.  “Oh dear,” she said simply.

Elliott followed her gaze to the window.  Hail the size of gumballs was raining down on the cars in the parking lot.  Elliott just stood and watched it hitting the pavement and bouncing back up six to twelve inches and suddenly was glad he hadn’t left yet, since his method of transportation was an old ten-speed bicycle.

Walking around his conveyor station, Elliott sighed again and bent over to retrieve the woman’s change.  He wondered how long he would have to stay at the store while the storm rolled over the area.  Texas weather may change fast, but there were some storms that just seem to favor a place and hang around for a good long while.

The sound of the hail seemed to get louder.  As Elliott was standing to look, he heard a distant but building sound of a siren.  

What now, he wondered, and then so many things happened in such quick succession that time tried to compensate by moving everything into a type of surreal slow motion.  As Elliott stood, he saw that the hail was much larger, realized the siren was the weather warning station at the edge of town, saw the panic-stricken face of a man running along the outside windows, looking over his shoulder, and then the sound of a rushing freight train deafened him as he saw cars being lifted in the parking lot, spinning and crashing against each other, one of them smashing into the running man as he reached the doors of the store.  Elliott saw blood on the glass doors which were trying to open, then saw the entire car coming through the front wall, the windows blowing out.  Suddenly, he was getting wet.  He looked up to see the roof peeling off the building.  The last thing he saw was the outside wall leaning in and bricks flying off the top as if being pinged by the finger of an angry giant.

Mercifully, he felt nothing.

Hal Nixon rubbed his eyes and shook his head.  That storm had hit fast and furious, and he was certain a tornado had hit the store, but things were bright now.  Amazing how quickly these things passed.  He looked around him.  It was hard to make out his surroundings exactly.  He rubbed his eyes again.  Where exactly was the store?  Slightly ahead of him and to his left, a woman walked slowly, looking around.  When she saw him, she jumped, her eyes wide.

“Where is everything?” she asked, her voice nearly as small as she was.  She looked like a left-over flower child, small, wearing a nearly floor-length skirt, her long straight hair hanging down her back.

Hal shrugged.  He ran his hand over his thinning hair and stopped.  “I don’t know,” he said weakly.

“Hey!”

Hal turned at the sound.  Another woman was walking up behind them.  Wearing a navy blue business suit and heels, she was frowning as she marched quickly up to them.

“What’s going on here?” she demanded.  I was just in the store and there was a commotion and now. . . “  She looked around angrily.  “Where is the store?”

Hal and the other woman looked at her blankly.

Just then a young man joined them, seemingly from out of nowhere.  “Do you guys have any idea what’s happening?” he asked.  “Was there a tornado?”

Hal nodded.  “I think so,” he said.  “But. . . I just have no idea. . .”

The small woman continued to look around timidly.  “Did we get blown out of the store?” she asked.

They all looked at her with raised eyebrows, but no one had an answer.  

They were standing in a field or a yard which alone was strange since the Handy Dandy was in the center of town.  The grass was high, but only we-should-have-mowed-it-a-few-days-ago high.  The area was fairly open, some trees interspersed throughout, but none too close by.  Pockets of pink, violet, and yellow wildflowers dotted the field.

“What’s that up ahead?” the woman in the heels said.

The other three looked and saw a house in the distance, a few hundred yards off. Hal slowly turned in a circle, checking the horizon.  There was simply nothing else around them.  Shrugging again, he began walking toward the house, the other three following him automatically.

The house was a two-story country farmhouse.  It was yellow with white trim and a porch which ran the length of the house.  There were white shutters at each paned window, two sets on each level of the house.  The yard around it was manicured neatly, flowers blooming profusely around two large trees in the front yard, along the walk leading up to the porch steps, and in large multi-colored arrays on either side of the steps, stretching the length of the porch.  Rather than neat little individual patches of flowers, the gardens were a cacophony of color and size and texture.  There was no one in sight.

The four stopped at the bottom of the porch steps.

“This is weird,” the young man said, echoing in one way or another everyone’s thoughts.

“Want me to knock?” Hal asked, looking at the others.

The small woman simply raised her eyebrows.  The young man and high-heeled woman shrugged, the man gesturing a go-ahead toward the door.

Hal swallowed, then climbed the stairs.  As he was lifting his hand to rap on the door, he saw a paper which had apparently been shut into the door.  Looking back over his shoulder first, he gripped the paper and tugged lightly.  It pulled easily from the door.  When he read it, he turned to the others, his face a picture of confusion.

“What?” the heeled woman demanded.

“Well,” Hal said, looking back down at the small paper, “it says, ‘Come in and make yourselves at home.’”

The three looked at each other, then back up at Hal.

“Us?” asked the young man.  “I mean, . . . Well, I don’t know what I mean.”

“Oh look,” said the woman in the suit suddenly, “I seem to have dropped my cell phone and I simply have to call my station.  I say let’s pretend the note’s for us and go in.  We can always plead ignorance later.”  She was already marching up the steps.

Hal’s face stretched into an expression of uncertainty, but stepped aside as the woman approached, and allowed her to open the door and go in.  The others hesitantly followed.

The interior of the house wasn’t exactly what the foursome expected.  Rather than the quaint country style anticipated, the inside was much more contemporary.  There was no foyer, but rather the door opened into the living area, a large room with black, soft leather furnishings on a cherry hardwood floor.  The room spanned the length of the house.  A stairway went up from the left of the door.  They could see only as far as the first landing.  Books lined shelves built into the right wall, small sculptures and other pieces or art interspersed among the books.  In the center of the shelves was a type of alcove holding what looked like a state-of-the-art stereo system.  At the back of the room was a large arched doorway that opened into a kitchen and dining room combination.  The lighter colors of that room reflected the light in such a way that it appeared almost to glow.

“Cool,” the young man said simply and walked toward the stereo.  The small woman moved toward the carpeted stairs and began creeping up them, calling tentatively, “Hello?” The other woman’s heels clumped against the floor as she headed for the kitchen, apparently in search of a telephone.  Hal stood just inside the door, looking around him.  A large black leather couch, two matching overstuffed wing-back chairs facing it, took up the center of the room.  The furniture was placed around an oversized round coffee table, wrought iron and glass, which sat on a small area rug with a woven geometric pattern of beiges and blacks.  Suddenly exhausted, Hal walked over to the wingback chair nearest to him, stepped around it, then dropped down into it, his back now to the door and the stairway.  The chair felt as if it had been made for him.  Sighing, he sank down into it, stretching his legs out in front of him.

“Well, there is no phone in the kitchen,” the woman said sharply, her hand on her hip as he marched back into the room.  “Is there one in here?”

“Huh?” asked the young man turning away from the stereo.

“A. Phone,” she said pointedly.  “Do you see a phone?”

The young man looked from side to side around him.  “Guess not,” he said, then went back to looking through what appeared to be an extensive collection of compact discs.  The woman sighed, dropped down on the couch across from Hal and frowned at him.  “I have to call my station,” she said.

“I know,” he said, trying to sound sympathetic.

After a moment, the young man said, almost to himself, “These are like my favorite cds.”

“What?” the woman barked.

The young man looked over his left shoulder at her.  “These cds.  Like every one of my favorites is in here.  That’s wild.”

The woman looked at Hal, lifted one eyebrow, and shook her head, rolling her eyes slightly.  Hal just shrugged and leaned back in the chair.  He could sleep here.  A minute later he was startled when from behind him he heard a small voice say, “Candace?”  The petite woman had come back down the stairs and was standing behind him.

The woman on the couch frowned.  “Yes?” she said impatiently.

“Hey,” Hal said, turning in the chair and looking around the side, “how did you know her name?”

Before she could answer, the woman on the couch said, “She recognized me, of course.  I’m a news reporter for KKTV.”  

The look on the small woman’s face caused Hal to not look away from her.  Her already light complexion was very pale now, her eyes so wide he could almost see white all the way around them.  A redness had begun on her throat and seemed to be creeping up her neck.  

“Ma’am,” he said, “are you all right?”

Her eyes slowly met his.  “Are you Harold,” she asked, “or Elliott?”

Hal’s eyes widened and he slowly stood up from the chair.  He glanced over at the young man by the stereo who was now staring at the obviously frightened woman.

“Uh, well, my name is Harold,” he said.  “People call me Hal,” he added, feeling foolish.  “But how--?”

She looked over at the young man.  “And you are Elliott?”

He nodded, silent.

She turned her ever-reddening face to the woman on the couch.  “I don’t watch television,” she said.  “I’ve never seen you before.”

Candace’s face went from annoyed to a delayed surprise.  “So then how--?”

“I think,” said the woman, “you guys need to come upstairs.”  Then she turned and walked back up the stairs.  The others all hesitated just a fraction of a second, then bolted from their positions to follow her.

There were eight steps up to the landing, which then took a 45-degree turn to the right, another ten stairs completing the climb to the second floor.  They were at the end of the hall.  At the far end was an open door, a tile floor and shelves visible, obviously a restroom.  On each side of the carpeted hall were two closed bedroom doors.  The small woman stepped quickly over to the first door on the right.  She pointed to a small sign positioned on the door at eye level.

“This is me,” she said quietly.  

The other three moved to look.  The sign on the door said, “Ariadne.”  They looked at her quizzically and she waved her hand down the hall, inviting them to look.

Before long there was a chorus of “heys” as each of the other three found a door with their name on it.  As shocking as even that would have been, the signs were not mere paper markers.  Permanently affixed to each door were bronze plaques, each engraved with one of their names.  The four turned away from the doors and stared at each other.  Finally Elliott once again essentially voiced everyone’s thoughts.

“Okay,” he said, “this is really starting to freak me out.”

Chapter 2

“Oh, this is ridiculous!”   Candace Richards was the first to exclaim, quickly opening the door with her name on it and bursting into the room.

She stood, dumbfounded, looking around, her mouth uncharacteristically opened in shock.  The room was perfect.  Better than her bedroom in her condo, it was the picture of what she was trying for with each shopping trip and on-line catalog.  The colors were muted mauves and violets with ivory accents.  The bed was an oversized four-poster in a rich, dark wood colors.  The pictures, in simple black or silver frames, were Ansel Adams replicas in black and white.  There was a desk pulled away from one wall, a high-back swivel office chair behind it.  The desk appeared to hold complete office supplies and a laptop computer.  No phone.

Rushing to the laptop, she turned it on.  It fired up immediately, the icons boasting of her favorite music site, word processing program, and photos.  Curious, she checked the photo program and found all the photos she had uploaded at home.  There was, however, no internet connection.  Candace frowned, leaning back in the chair and swiveling to look at the room.  What was this place. . . ?

Next to her room, at the stairway end of the hall, Harold “Hal” slowly wandered through the room apparently designated for him.  He was amazed at how comfortable he felt in the room, although, at 45, he could not remember ever having designed or arranged a room himself.  Going from his parents house to the one he shared with his wife, he had always gladly left decorating decisions up to the women in his life.  But, he conceded, if he had designed a room. . . this would be it.  The furniture was dark, the colors rich.  There was a stereo and a wall-to-wall bookshelf lined with leather-bound books and records.  Not cds, records.  And no modern technology anywhere.  No computer or this-pad or that-phone.  Just books and records.  Hal smiled and started toward the shelves to check titles, but he knew he was going to like what he saw.

Across the hall, Elliott Prescott let out a squeal of delight.  Smiling large, turning in a circle and looking around “his” room, he held his arms out in joy.  For at least half of his twenty-four years, Elliott had dreamed of being a musician.  Well, actually, he argued, he was a musician, it was just that no one else seemed to know that yet.  The room he was standing in was beyond his greatest imagination.  Lining one wall was a series of guitars, electric and acoustic, all his favorite types, some of which he would never have dreamed of handling.  In the corner was a large and very powerful amp system.

Barely noticing the futon, apartment fridge, and small stereo with cds, Elliott picked up the Fender twelve-string and walked, as if mesmerized, over to the amp.  He plugged the cord into the guitar, cranked up the dial on the amp, and with a wicked grin, hit the strings, a G-chord blasting with perfect clarity out of the speakers.  He did a quick run down the frets and never squawked a note, even when his fingers on the frets and on the strings were so close he had to maneuver his hands to fit them both on.  When he stopped, he grinned again.  That was the loudest he’d played in a while and his ears weren’t even numb.

This place rocks, Elliott thought, dropping happily into the nearby papasan chair, hugging the Fender up against him.

Ariadne Greene sat on the edge of the bed, her heels hooked on the bed frame, her hands on her knees.  A tear coursed slowly down her cheek.  Although she didn’t look her years, Ariadne was nearly fifty, and had pretty much decorated her home just the way she wanted it.  Having lived alone for much of her adult life, she had the opportunities and resources to make any decision she cared to about the décor of her home.  Her bedroom was exactly as she wanted it, earthy, sparse, comfortable, understated.  The room she sat in now was nothing like her real room.  And this room was perfect.

Ariadne looked around her in wonder.  The room was bright yellow, with fabrics in pastel florals, delicate and lacy.  The bed was brass with simple rounded knobs.  There was a corner with a white wicker rocker, a colorful throw draped across the back.  On the floor next to it was a large wicker basket filled with yarns in nearly every color.  In the corner by the window was a large easel, a table next to it holding a large supply of paints, brushes, cloths, and other artist supplies.  

Overcome by the beauty and the perfection of the room, Ariadne pulled her feet up on the bed, hugging her knees against her chest, another tear sliding down her face, her eyes glistening.

Eventually, the four wandered back into the hall of the second floor.  There was silence until Elliott came out of his room apologizing.

“I know that was loud,” he said, “but I’ve never played on one of those before and I—“

“What are you talking about?” Candace interrupted him.

“The guitar,” he said, pointing with his thumb over his shoulder.  “It was—“

“We didn’t hear anything,” she said, frowning at him.

Elliott’s eyebrows went up.  Whoa, he thought, great soundproofing.

Hal cleared his throat.  “Let’s, uh, go downstairs,” he said, “and try to make some sense of all this.”

As they walked down the stairs, Candace said, “Did anyone find a phone in their rooms?”  Silence answered her.

When they reached the living room, Hal and Ariadne automatically took the chairs, Candace and Elliott moving right to the couch.  Elliott put his feet up on the glass-topped table, not noticing the disapproving glance from Candace.

“So,” Elliott said, smiling, “what is this place?”

Candace rolled her eyes.  At thirty-one, she knew she wasn’t the oldest here, but she sure felt like it.  Brushing her blonde hair back from her face, she said, “Obviously, none of us knows what this place is,” she said.  “Were we all in the store when the tornado hit?”

Ariadne pulled her waist-length hair around her shoulder and let it drape down the front of her.  Too small for her feet to touch the floor, she curled her legs sideways in the chair.  “The Handy Dandy, right?”

Everyone nodded.

“I work there,” Elliott said.  Then, his voice smaller, added, “I saw a guy get killed by a car, then the roof coming off, then. . . Well, then I was here.”

There was silence for a moment, then Hal said, “I was in the back, at the deli.  I saw the doors to the back room blow in, toward the store.  At least two guys in the white aprons came flying through.  I don’t think they. . . Well. . .”

Ariadne had leaned around the wingback side of her chair to watch Hal.  She nodded, knowingly.  “I saw an older couple looking for a place to go… for protection.  Just as they went around an aisle, the shelves fell toward them.”

Candace was frowning.  “I saw a mother grabbing up her two little kids.  When the roof started to go, I didn’t see them anymore.   Then,” she added, looking around at the others, “then I was here.”

There was silence again.

“Are we dead?”  It was Elliott.

Hal frowned and shook his head.  “I don’t feel dead,” he said.  “I mean, not sure how that does feel, but wouldn’t there be pearly gates, streets of gold, and angels?”

Ariadne was watching him intently.  “’To be absent from the body is to be present with the Lord,’” she quoted.  “I don’t see the Lord anywhere here.”

“Certainly not,” Candace chimed in, her legs crossed and one foot swinging almost violently.  “This is a nice house, but it’s certainly not Heaven.  There’s not even internet.”

“You think there’s internet in Heaven?” Ariadne asked, no ridicule in her tone.

Candace shrugged impatiently. “There’s no way to contact anyone or find out anything – no television, phones, internet. . .”

“Maybe it’s purgatory,” Elliott suggested, his eyes wide.  

Candace made a sound of disgust.  “I don’t believe in purgatory,” she said, dismissing the idea.  “And besides, isn’t that supposed to be a bad place?”

Elliott shrugged.  

Finally, it was Ariadne who voiced the question they were avoiding.  “What are we going to do?” she asked.  

After an awkward silence, Hal placed his hands on his knees and pushed himself up.  Just over six feet, his 220 pounds more excess than toned, he looked around at the others.  “I’m going to go outside,” he said.  “I’m going to see what else is around here.  You all stay here.”

“No!” Ariadne said, surprising everyone.  “You can’t leave us here!”

Hal looked at her.  “I’m coming back,” he said patiently.  “I’ll come back and let you know whatever I found.  We can’t make a decision without information.”

 The other three looked at each other, then back at Hal who waited.  Finally, Candace said, “Well, we don’t appear to have any better ideas, so go ahead.”

“Be careful,” Ariadne added.

Elliott stood up.  “Should I go with you?” he asked.

Hal shook his head.  “No, I think you should wait here.  I’ll be back.”

The women stood up and all of them followed Hal to the door and onto the front porch.  Stretching, he looked around.  It was a beautiful day.  The sun was bright without being blinding, occasional clouds dotting the sky, a very slight breeze keeping the air cool.  Perfect day.

“We came from that way,” he said, pointing.  “So I’ll try going that way.”  He pointed to the back of the house.  He turned and looked at the three others.  “Any of you guys Christians?” he asked.

Three sets of eyebrows went up at the question.  

“Well, yeah,” Elliott said, nodding.

Ariadne blushed and nodded.

Candace frowned and crossed her arms.  “I don’t know what that has to do with anything.  But yes, I am, why?”

Hal nodded.  “Me too.   So pray.  Pray we can figure this out and hopefully get back to our lives and families.”

There were silent nods of agreement, the three staying on the porch and watching as Hal walked down the steps and around the side of the house, quickly going out of sight.  The three sighed and, turning, went back into the house.  Ariadne dropped back into the chair, Candace paced the room, and Elliott went to the stereo.  

“You guys mind if I put on some music?” he asked.

The women shrugged.

Thinking twice, Candace opened her mouth to suggest it not be too loud, but Elliott had already placed the cd and was pushing the button.  Clamping her mouth shut in frustration, she was soon surprised to hear the sounds coming from the stereo.  It was an easy jazz tune, heavy on piano and bass.  Elliott adjusted the sound and the bass came out more clearly.

“One day, I’ll be able to play like that,” he muttered.  As he turned away from the system, he looked at the door, surprised.  The women followed his gaze.

Hal was walking in the door, his face flushed.

“Back so soon?” Candace said.

Hal’s face registered shock.  “’So soon’?” he said.  “What do you mean ‘so soon’?  I’ve been gone for hours!”

Chapter 3

Back in the living room, all in the same positions as before, it didn’t take Hal long to tell his story.  He had walked, using what he knew from his boy scout days – the sun, the trees ahead, the house behind – to make sure he walked in as straight a line as possible.  After awhile he could no longer see the house and there was nothing ahead of him.  He kept walking, amazed that as out-of-shape as he was he never got winded or tired, determined not to turn back until he found something.  Finally he saw a building up ahead.  Picking up the pace, he walked more quickly than he thought he could toward the structure.

It was the yellow house again.

“It’s like we’re the only thing on a very small world,” he said, his legs stretched out in front of him.  “There was nothing, nothing else out there.”  He looked around at the other three.  “Just us,” he added, knowing how ominous it sounded.  “I walked and walked and ended up back here.”

“And yet,” Ariadne added quietly, “very little time had passed for us.  We had just come back inside and sat down when you walked in the door.”

Hal’s eyes widened.  

“Freaky,” Elliott said.

There was a long silence, then Elliott added, “Maybe this is Purgatory.”

Candace rolled her eyes.

Pulling her feet up into the chair, hugging her arms around her knees, Ariadne said, her voice even smaller than before, “I’m getting scared.”

Hal frowned over at her.  “There’s just no . . . I don’t know, there’s no explanation for all this!  I mean, it’s like we’re trapped here, yet I don’t feel too. . .” he gestured with his hands, “worried, I guess.  It’s nice here.  I don’t feel. . . I don’t know, threatened.  I mean, I want to get out, but not because I feel. . .” He shook his head helplessly.  Looking over at Ariadne, he said, “Do you really feel scared?”

She’d been frowning thoughtfully as he talked.  Slowly shaking her head, she hugged her knees closer.  “Actually, no.  I think I just thought I should be scared.  I’m, well. . . “

“Freaked out?” Elliott suggested.

Ariadne smiled.  “Yes,” she agreed.  “Yes, freaked out pretty well defines it.”

Candace stood up suddenly.  “Well, let’s see if it’s heaven after all,” she said, and headed around the couch toward the kitchen.

“How’s that?” Hal asked.

Without looking back, she said, “I’m hungry.  Let’s go see if the fridge is full of milk and the cabinets full of honey.”

Several minutes later, they sat back in the living room, each with a plate holding some of their favorite foods.  It hadn’t surprised them to find the refrigerator and pantry filled with their individual top choices.  On a whim, Elliott had checked the freezer and found each person’s favorite ice cream.  To his great delight, Hal had found a coffee maker and immediately began brewing a large pot of dark coffee.  

He picked the mug up from the table beside the chair and drank a large mouthful of the brew.  It was the best coffee he’d ever had.  Might be heaven, he thought, taking another swallow.

“What is that you’re eating?” Candace said to Ariadne.

Swallowing, the little woman looked down at her plate, then up at Candace on the couch.  “Um, fruit, cheese, hummus, wheat crackers….”

Elliott took another mouthful of his roast beef on white bread sandwich, then said, his voice muffled by the food, “You wan’ some of my beef?”

Ariadne’s face registered mild disgust.  “I don’t eat meat,” she said.

Candace tipped her head, one eyebrow up.  “Well, I prefer salads and fruit myself, but I never turn down a good fillet.”

“Amen,” Hal said absently.

“Steak?” Ariadne said, her mild disgust apparently growing.  “Cow?”

Elliott was in mid-bite and looked quickly from Candace next to him to Hal, his eyebrows up and eyes smiling.  “Moo,” he said as he sunk his teeth into the sandwich.

Ignoring him, Candace said, “What’s wrong with steak?”

Ariadne, looking repulsed now, sat her plate down on the table between her chair and Hal’s.  “It’s a living thing,” she said, shaking her head.  “It has a heart and a brain and feelings.  Do you know what their living conditions are like?  Do you have any idea what a slaughterhouse is like?”

Candace looked amused, but Hal frowned.  “I’ve actually thought of that,” he said.  “But it’s easy to not think of it when you’re ordering a 20-ounce rib-eye.”

Turning slightly to better look at him, Ariadne said, “Don’t you ever worry about karma?”

“Karma!” Candace practically spat out the word.  “What kind of new age thinking is that?”

“Karma is anything but new,” Ariadne said.  “It’s a universal belief of cause and effect.  What happens to us is caused by our own actions.  And those actions create other affects for ourselves, our lives, and our very world.  Eating a living being… that just creates a very negative karma that is already coming back on us with our high cholesterol, high blood pressure, weight problems, heart problems.  One day we all pay for our bad choices.”

  “Well now, I agree with that part,” Hal said, patting his stomach almost absently.  “I’ve made plenty of bad choices that I’ve paid for.  But I think that’s all it is – bad choices.  Not ‘karma.’  Isn’t that Buddhist or something?”

Ariadne nodded.  “It plays a part in Buddhism as well as other eastern religions.  I told you I’m a Christian, and I wasn’t kidding, but I think a concept like karma is universal, not just limited to the religions that call it by that name.  We call it bad choices, bad luck, coincidence, destiny.  But it all means the same thing.”

There was silence for a moment, then Candace, still staring at her, said, “Were you given the name Ariadne at birth or did you change your name?”

Not sure how to take the question, Ariadne looked at her for a moment, then said, “My mother gave it to me.”

“Was she a flower child?” Candace asked, her gaze not shifting.  Elliott and Hal began to squirm uncomfortably.

Ariadne looked uneasy, then swallowed and sat up straighter in the chair, brushing her skirt down over her legs.  When she spoke, her voice was clear.  “Yes, she was.  My mother was a rebel from a young age.  She spoke her mind when women weren’t expected or allowed to do so.  She took nothing for granted and accepted no answer without investigating it herself first.  She was very serious about exploring her feelings and wouldn’t do anything until she knew exactly how she felt about it.  She embraced the hippie culture for its freedom and openness.”

The others sat in stunned silence.  Ariadne looked almost as if she no longer remembered they were there.  She was looking off into the distance now.  Her face was set, serious.  It didn’t appear that the memories she was recalling were necessarily pleasant ones.  “She had me alone, also a thing not done back then.  She raised me alone.  Just as I raised  my daughter alone.  Just as she is raising my granddaughter alone.”

There was a long silence.  Ariadne cleared her throat, wiped her eyes, brushed her skirt down again.  “Karma,” she said simply.

The first to speak was Elliott, in a quiet voice.  “You must really miss them a lot,” he said.  “Your daughter and granddaughter.”

Ariadne smiled over at him, her eyes glistening.  “I’ve missed them for a long time,” she said simply.  “My daughter doesn’t necessarily embrace the same ideas I do.”

After an awkward moment, Ariadne said, “How about you, Elliott?  Who have you left behind?”

Everyone in the room was rocked by the question.  All of them had been thinking it, but no one had ventured to discuss it or bring it out in the open.  Elliott seemed so shocked by the question, he didn’t know how to answer.

After a slight, awkward silence, Candace said loudly, an edge of anger in her voice, “Well now, that just brings up the elephant in the room, doesn’t it?”

Ariadne, unfazed, looked over at her.  “It had to come up eventually, Candace.  We don’t know how long we’re going to be here or if we’re ever getting back.”

Hal looked, surprised, at Ariadne.  Little lady has more spunk than I gave her credit for, he thought.  Clearing his throat, he said, “I left a wife of twenty-one years and two kids.  My daughter Melanie, Mellie, is sixteen.  My son, Steven, is nineteen.  They both live at home.”  He hung his arm up over the winged side of the chair, his hand higher than his head.  “That gives me some comfort,” he said.  “They live at home and maybe their mom won’t be so--  That is, will maybe handle all this better.”

Elliott sat his plate down on the coffee table, his sandwich unfinished.  “I had a girlfriend,” he said.  “Her name is Stormy and she’s the best.  Plays the most wicked drums you’ve ever heard.  Skinny little thing, but we’ve never had to mike the drums when we did a gig together.  She’s amazing.”  He paused.  “But she’s beautiful and talented.  She’ll find someone else.  My Dad is mister tough guy.  My mom, though, . . . She’s going to take this hard.”

Candace slammed her cup of coffee down on the table in front of her, the last of the coffee sloshing up and over the side.  Ignoring it, she barked, “Going to take what hard, Elliott?  And,” she said angrily, looking at Hal, “your wife is going to handle what better?  We don’t know what this is!” she said, each word punctuated as if it were a sentence all its own.  “You people are sitting here reading off your own obituaries and we don’t even know that we’re dead!  I don’t feel dead!  Do dead people drink coffee?  Do dead people eat real food?”  She poked her fingers into her shoulders and chest.  “I can feel, my body is real!  We even have to go to the bathroom, for goodness sake!  Do you really think they have plumbing in the afterlife?”

There was another stunned silence, interrupted by a giggle.  The other three all turned and looked at Elliott, his hand over his mouth, his eyes sparkling.  He dropped his hand down from his smiling mouth.  “Well, she’s got a point,” he said.  “Of all the things I expect to do in heaven, going potty isn’t one of them.  I mean, seems like that would be sort of taken care of, you know?  I mean—“

“Okay, Elliott,” Hal said, smiling and waving his hand.  “Okay.  We get it.”  Looking at Candace, he said, “Fact is, Candace -- Can I call you Candy?”

Looking aghast, she said, “Absolutely not.”

Eyebrows lifted, Hal nodded and said, “Okay, Candace, fact is we don’t have any way of knowing where we are.  There doesn’t seem to be much we can do about it.  But it also appears that all of our comforts have been considered and met, so. . .”

“So what?” she said, crossing her arms and legs, one foot swinging.

“So, I guess we just, well, wait.”

“Wait?” she said, her foot swinging faster.  “Wait for what?”

Hal cleared his throat, looked to the other two for support.  He shifted uncomfortably in the chair.  “Wait. . . well, until.” He shrugged, his arms out to the side.  “I don’t know until what or for what.  But it seems we’re meant to be here, I think we all believe God has something to do with it, so we just wait here until He shows us what it’s all about.  What this is all about.  Why we’re. . . here,” he finished weakly, his arms dropping down.  “If you think about it,” he added, “it’s not like we have much choice.  I don’t see anything else we can do.  Do you?”

Candace was staring at him, her arms still crossed, her foot still bouncing.  Her face had begun to turn red and a strain had come into her eyes.  Ariadne leaned forward in her chair.  “Candace, are you okay?”

Turning her head rapidly toward Ariadne, Candace practically yelled, “Yes!  I’m just fine!”  Standing suddenly, without another word, she swept up her plate and mug and marched quickly into the kitchen.

The men looked at Ariadne who gazed toward the kitchen, her eyes soft with sympathy.  “She was about to cry,” she whispered.  “I don’t think she does that often.  Or well.”

Elliott looked frightened.  “What should we do?” he asked in a stage whisper.

“Nothing,” Ariadne whispered back, smiling.

The sound of Candace’s heals got louder and they looked up to find her walking back in the room, her face once again composed.  “Okay,” she said, “so what do we do around here for entertainment?”  Sitting back on the couch, she turned to Elliott.  “How about turning on some more of that music of yours?”

Elliott frowned and looked at her as if he hadn’t understood her.  Then he looked slowly over at Hal and Ariadne.  He lifted up his hand and looked at it like he’d never seen it before.  His eyes widened as he turned his hand in front of his face.

“Elliott?” Hal said.  “You okay?”

“I feel. . . weird,” Elliott said, running his hands up and down his chest, then down his legs, then up and down his arms.  “I feel like I’m. . . “ He looked up, his face a picture of confusion.  “I just feel weird.”

“Hey!” Candace had been looking at Elliott and now was jumping up from the couch.  “Hey!” she said again, pointing at him.

Hal and Ariadne stood quickly, staring at Elliott, afraid to approach him.  He seemed to be slowly disappearing right before their eyes.  As they stared, he began to look less three-dimensional and more like a fading photograph.  They gasped and Ariadne reached her hands out toward him as he became so transparent the couch could be seen through him.

“What’s happening?” Elliott said.

Then he disappeared all together.

Chapter 4

“Sorry there, hun, I didn’t see you come in.  What can I get you?”

Elliott looked toward the voice, then around him.  He was sitting on a barstool at a counter in what appeared to be a bar or nightclub.  A middle-aged woman with bright red hair pinned into a piled up-do and matching bright red lipstick was eyeing him curiously from the other side of the bar.  

“You okay there, hun?”

Elliott opened his mouth, but could only clamp it shut again.  He held his hands up in front of him, turning them slowly.  They looked normal again.  Wow, he thought, what a rush!  As he sat in the living room with the other three, he had begun to feel fuzzy and lightheaded.  His body had gotten tingly and numb.  Then he had felt as if he was losing consciousness.  But before he fully passed out, he began to feel less tingly and numb, more focused, and now here he sat, in this unknown place with the woman now frowning at him suspiciously, her head tipped to one side, one raised eyebrow threatening to disappear into her hairline.

“I, uh,” he cleared his throat and shook his head, the last of the tingles gone.  “Can I get, um, a ginger ale?”

The woman continued to look at him for another moment, sizing up his sanity, no doubt, he thought, then slowly nodded her head.

“Ginger ale.  Right.”  She turned and walked away, down the length of the bar.

Elliott looked around.  The place was dim, but he could make out several tables behind him, booths on the opposite wall, the windows above them tinted slightly.  Elliott could make out the backwards painting on the windows and reversed it in his head.  “Gerda’s,” it proclaimed simply.  There was only one couple sitting at one of the booths and an older man hunched over a glass at one of the tables.  With Elliott’s back to the bar, the door was to his left.  At the far end of the room, to his right, was a stage.  There was an electric keyboard, an acoustic guitar leaning in a stand, a drum set, and three mikes on stands.  Elliott tipped his head curiously.  

“Open mike night.”  It was the red-headed woman, back with his ginger ale.  “You a musician?”

Elliott’s eyes widened.  “Well. . . yes,” he said.  “Um…”

The woman leaned her elbows on the bar and peered at Elliott.  “You never been here before, have you.”

“No,” he said, picking up the drink and taking a sip.  “Um, where is this exactly?” He inwardly cringed at how the question must sound.  Sure enough, the woman’s eyebrow went up into her red hair again.

“Well, now, hun, you are in Gerda’s.  I’m Gerda.” She smiled at him, but the smile was more pitying than kind.  “Welcome.”

Elliott took another swallow of the ginger ale.  “Thanks.”  He tried to think of a way to word what he wanted to ask without her calling authorities carrying long-sleeved white jackets.  “Um,” he began again, “and this place is. . . where?”

Now both eyebrows went up.  “You mean what street?  You don’t know what street you’re on?  Honey, you been to how many other bars today?”

Elliott deflated a little.  What did he have to lose?  “Actually, I was wondering what city this is.”

Gerda stood up straight, stared at him a moment, then bent again with her elbows on the bar.  She was wearing a scoop-necked sweater, too much of her showing when she leaned forward like that.  Elliott kept his eyes on her face, heavily made up in an attempt to hide the lines of age that were beginning to show.  Her look was one of amused curiosity.

“I’ve been in this business a long time,” she said.  “And that question is a first for me.” She smiled, her mouth tilting slightly to one side.  “Dallas, hun.  Texas.  Dallas, Texas.”

Elliott felt a surge of electricity.  So close to home!  “What day is it?” he said, excitement in his voice.

Gerda just shook her head, still smiling.  “Thursday, Hun. May—“

“Thursday!”  Elliott looked around him eagerly.  “Do you have a phone I can use?”

Gerda continued to eye him like an amusing zoo exhibit.  “The pay phone has been broken forever.  Store phone is on the fritz.  Must be that storm system moving in.”

The storm!  “How about a cell phone?”

Gerda jerked her thumb over her right shoulder without turning around.  Elliott leaned slightly to look around her.  A hand-drawn sign on the wall said, “No cell phone zone.”

“Hate those things,” Gerda said.

Elliott frowned, then realized that he didn’t know what he would have done with a phone anyway.  Call who?  About what?  As Gerda patted his arm and walked slowly away again, Elliott frowned and took another swallow of the soda.  It was the same day as the storm.  The storm hadn’t hit yet.  How was that possible?  Elliott shook his head.  So many unexplainable things had happened lately, that particular question had become a waste of time.  Elliott looked around again.  He was put here.  Of that he was certain.  So why was he here?  Was it to warn people of the incoming storm?  If that was the reason, wouldn’t he be back in the Handy Dandy?  Elliott frowned again.  Maybe this place had been hard hit by the storm, too.  Was he their warning system?  Just these four people?

Elliott sighed and looked around anxiously.  It was even dimmer in the bar now.  The storm clouds must be moving in.  Elliott bit his lower lip.  Eyeing the door, he wondered if he should leave.  Maybe he was supposed to go back to the store.  But why then wasn’t he just “placed” in the store?  Maybe he was being given a chance to go home and start over.  Maybe he wasn’t meant to be at the store during the storm and this was his chance to have a re-do on his day.  Maybe—

“Hey!” He jumped at the sound.  It was Gerda from the other end of the bar, nearest the stage.  When he looked at her, she was waving at him.  Pointing to the stage, she said, “It’s boring in here today.  We need some excitement.  You’re a musician, why don’t you come entertain us?”

Elliott’s heart jumped.  And suddenly he knew.  This was it.  This was why he was here.  He looked around at the people in the bar.  Still just three customers.  If his music and his performance were somehow of great importance… Elliott frowned as he looked at the people.  Wouldn’t there be a roomful of people?  How was playing for four tired people important enough to place him here.  Unless, he thought suddenly, his heart pounding, unless one of them was an agent!  Or even knew an agent!

Elliott felt his heart surge as he stood up from the bar stool.  But as he started to walk toward the stage, he realized his heart had only surged as far as his throat and lodged there, threatening to choke him.  His palms suddenly sweaty, Elliott’s steps slowed and his walk became more unsteady as he continued haltingly toward the stage.  His ears began to roar.  

What’s all this? he wondered as he tried to keep putting one foot in front of the other.  He eyed the guitar and willed himself to keep walking toward it.  His stomach began to cramp.

Worst case of stage fright ever, he thought, as he continued slowly toward the stage.  An audience of four and I feel like I’m walking up to the mike at like Madison Square Garden or something.  Elliott continued to chastise himself as he moved forward by inches.  Then suddenly it hit him.

He had no idea what to sing.

Elliott stopped.  His face began to burn as he realized his mind was totally blank.  Over the years he’d written several songs, some of them even pretty good.  Mostly bluesy numbers, easy jazz sound.  Gentle ballads about loneliness, love, hope – depending on his latest life crisis.  But right now, in this moment, he had no idea what to do.  He couldn’t think of a single one of his own songs.  Not one.

Gerda was smiling at him.  “So?” she said.  “Let’s hear what you got.”

Elliott stared at her.  Opened his mouth to speak.  Had no idea what to say.  Clamped his mouth shut again.

Gerda tipped her head, still grinning.  “C’mon!” she encouraged.  “We promise not to throw tomatoes!”

Suddenly, Elliott knew he was going to be sick.  He turned and ran back the length of the bar to the restrooms.  Crashing into the stall, he made it just in time.   He stood over the toilet, coughing and choking, tears burning his eyes, his humiliation and frustration complete.  A few moments later, leaning over the sink, splashing cool water on his face, Elliott barely had the strength to stand.  He looked up at himself in the mirror over the sink.  His image was distorted by the smudged and dirty mirror.  Elliott turned his head slowly back and forth, watching his face warp and alter.  He frowned.  

I wasn’t ready, he thought.  He realized he was praying.  You gave me a chance and I wasn’t ready.  Maybe I can get another cha—

Elliott had stopped turning his head.  Now his eyebrows furrowing when he realized his image was continuing to change.  He began to feel light-headed.  He flexed his hands.  They felt like they were going to sleep, all tingly and –

Elliott looked up suddenly at himself in the mirror.

“Uh oh.”

Hal was back in the wingback chair, staring again at the end of the couch where Elliott had been before he’d disappeared hours ago.  The three of them had made a frantic search in and around the house for the boy, all to no avail.  They had discussed for some time why he would have been “taken” and to where, until finally giving up in total frustration.  

“I am sick to death,” Candace had stated, “of asking questions with no answers.”

Finally, they had each just gone their own way for awhile – the ladies to their rooms, Hal to the porch.  After just a few minutes, Ariadne joined him silently on the porch.  Less than an hour later, Candace joined them as well, glancing guiltily at them as she positioned herself in a wicker rocker.  She was wearing a pair of jeans and loose-fitting purple sweater she’d found in the closet in her room.

Hal spoke for all of them:  “Hard not to kind of keep an eye on each other.”

The ladies nodded.

“Do you think we’ll. . . go away, too?”  It was Ariadne.

The newest unanswerable question just hung in the air as they watched the sun slowly setting.

As darkness descended, Candace said, “Well, at least we know time passes here.”  She stood, stretching slightly.  “I’m getting hungry.  Supper?”

Without answering, the other two stood and followed her into the house.

Supper finished, the three migrated back into the living room.  Hal sat staring at the empty end of the couch.  Candace was looking at one of the books she’d found on the shelf.  Ariadne had carried down the basket of yarn and, having sorted through the various colors, was creating the first line of chain stitches for a crochet project.  Hal had put on a cd of light classical music.  He didn’t know as much about music as he would like to, but he knew what he liked.  When he was in a contemplative mood, Bach was his favorite.  When he was on his way to work, he liked Mozart or even Rachmaninoff.  On the way home, it was usually Beethoven, especially the sonatas.  There was a lot he listened to that he didn’t even know the composer.  He just liked the sound and the way it made him feel.

Hal sighed and tipped his head back on the chair.  

Candace looked up at him as she turned another page that she hadn’t read.

Ariadne looked over, her hands automatically continuing the uncounted chain stitching.

Then the three of them jumped, a tiny scream escaping Ariadne, when there was a sudden and loud pounding on the stairs.  All three jumped up and turned to see Elliott practically jumping off the last step.  He was frowning slightly, his face intense, as he ran into the room.

“I wasn’t ready!” he all but shouted.  He rushed past them into the kitchen.

The three stared at each other, each mouth open in shock.  Ariadne had her hand to her mouth.  Before any of them could move or say anything, Elliott came back out of the kitchen.  He was carrying a cold bottle of water from the refrigerator.  

“I wasn’t ready,” he said again to his stunned audience.  “I was given a chance to perform and I wasn’t ready.”  He jabbed his finger toward Hal.  “It’s all about being prepared,” he said seriously.  “And I wasn’t prepared.  But that’s not going to happen again!”  He began walking again, heading quickly toward the steps.  “I’m going to get to work on the best song I’ve ever written.  And next time – and I’m somehow sure there will be a next time – I’ll be ready!”

Without another look at the three, Elliott pounded back up the stairs.  They heard his door slam shut just a moment later.

Candace, Ariadne, and Hal slowly turned and looked at each other, their eyes wide, mouths still open.

“Well,” Ariadne said simply, sitting unsteadily back down in the chair, bending to retrieve the long yarn chain that had fallen from her lap.

“Right,” Hal said, his knees a bit shaky as he too sat back into his chair.  

Candace dropped back onto the couch, the book still in her hands.  As she crossed one leg over the other and reopened the book, she muttered matter-of-factly, “And then there were four.  Again.”

Chapter 5

The next morning, when Ariadne walked down the stairs and into the living room, she found Elliott sitting on the couch, leaning over the guitar across his lap, and writing on a pad of paper which lay on the coffee table.  He looked up as she entered the room.  His eyes were red, dark circles under them, but he was smiling.

“Good morning!” he said brightly.

“Elliott,” she said as she slowly approached him, “are you all right?  Have you slept at all?”

Elliott shook his head.  “Nope.  Not last night.  Never know when I’m going to poof out of here again and I meant it when I said I was going to be ready!”

He was speaking very quickly, his words like rapid machine-gun fire.  Ariadne tipped her head and squinted her eyes.  “Are you all right?” she repeated.

“Oh sure!” he said, using the end of his pencil to tap a coffee mug sitting next to him on the table.  “I made coffee!  I’ve never made coffee before, but I was getting tired so I thought it would be a good idea so I just sort of figured it out.  I figured about one scoop per cup, doesn’t that sound about right?  Boy, that coffee is strong stuff!  I put in a ton of sugar, but it worked really good! I’ve not gotten sleepy since about two o’clock this morning!”

Ariadne’s eyes had slowly widened as he talked.  She didn’t envy him the crash he was going to have later when all that caffeine left his system.  

“And how is the song coming?” she asked, pulling the fluffy robe she’d found in her closet around her.

“Great!  Or pretty great anyway!” Elliott’s movements were jerky, his eyes unfocused.  “I’ve just started hitting a bit of a roadblock and thought maybe I needed to take a break but I just hate to, you know, since I sure don’t know when I might, you know,” he waved his hand in the air as if demonstrating the movement of smoke rolling up from a fire.

Ariadne smiled at him.  Walking around the table to his side of the couch, she rubbed his shoulder.  His muscles twitched under her touch.  

“Honey,” she said gently, “why don’t you take just a little break and let me fix you some toast to soak up all that coffee you drank.  Okay?”

Elliott looked up at her, but it was hard to tell if he was looking her directly in the eye.  He nodded, frowning slightly, laid the pencil down on the table, and surprised Ariadne when he handed the guitar to her.  Taking it by the neck, she walked to the wall of shelves and propped it gently up, taking care that it wouldn’t accidently topple over.

“Okay,” she said as she secured the guitar, “I’ll go make the –“  

When she turned to look at Elliott, he was already sound asleep, curled up on the couch like a baby.

“Wow,” Ariadne breathed to herself.  Shaking her head, she headed toward the kitchen to find breakfast for herself.

A few minutes later she was joined by Candace who walked into the kitchen wearing a bright blue warm-up suit and tennis shoes.  

“I see our happy wanderer crashed on the couch last night,” she said.

Ariadne smiled at her as she placed her toasted and buttered English muffin and mug of warm apple juice on the table.  “No, he spent the night on the couch, but he just now crashed.  Poor thing was hopped up on more caffeine than I’ve had in my entire life.”

Candace shook her head and laughed as she headed for the coffee maker to prepare a fresh pot.  As she poured out the little bit left in the decanter, she laughed even harder.  “This stuff is like tar!” she exclaimed, pouring the thick brew into the sink.  “How much coffee did he use?”

Ariadne sipped her apple juice.  “One scoop per cup,” she answered.  “Is that too much?”

Candace’s eyebrows shot up and she looked over at Ariadne, the pot still in her hand, hanging over the sink.

“Is that too much?  I like my coffee strong and I use one scant scoop for two to three cups.”

Ariadne grinned.  “Wow,” she said simply.

A minute later, the pot happily percolating, Candace grabbed a banana from a fruit bowl on the counter, and sat across from Ariadne.  Popping the stem to begin peeling the banana, she frowned over at the other woman’s cup.

“What’s that you’re drinking?” she asked.

“Apple juice.”

“Hot?”

“Mm,” Ariadne nodded as she took another sip.

Candace just stared at her.

“What?” Ariadne finally asked.

“Hot apple juice.”

Ariadne nodded and took another sip.  “Yes, it’s quite good.  Would you like some?”

“Heavens no.  Why, if I may ask, would you drink hot apple juice?”

Ariadne put the cup down and looked at Candace.  “It’s a very tasty and satisfying – and healthy – alternative for coffee.  Hot and stimulating, but no caffeine.”

Candace hadn’t moved or changed expression.  “Why not just decaf?”

 Ariadne met her gaze.  “Because there is no dietary or health benefit whatsoever to decaffeinated coffee.  And it’s not one hundred percent caffeine-free.  The apple juice has natural sugar as a morning stimulant, but no artificial ingredients or additives.”

The two stared at each other for another moment across the table.  Ariadne found herself wondering how long it would last and was grateful when Hal walked into the kitchen, causing them both to look over at him.

“Morning,” he said, frowning at the still-perking coffee pot.  “What’s with sleeping beauty on the couch?”

Ariadne told them both about her encounter with Elliott.  

Candace peeled the banana halfway down and took a small bite.  “So what’s your take on his idea about being prepared with a song?” she asked Hal.

Hal leaned back in the chair, crossed his arms over his chest.  Frowning, he said, “I have no idea.  But he knows what he felt, and I’m not sure I have any right to dispute that.”

“How does he know that was why he was ‘sent’ there?” Candace asked, sounding like a reporter.

Hal frowned at her.  “I don’t know,” he said, realizing how very tired he was of giving that answer.  “But he was pretty sure.  I suppose he felt like he’d gotten a word from God.”

Candace’s eyes widened.  Her expression was a mix of humor and skepticism.  

“What?” Ariadne asked her.  “You don’t think he could have gotten a word from God?”

Candace shrugged, an amused expression on her face.  “Well, I suppose….,” she said.

“Aren’t you a Christian?” Ariadne asked, leaning forward and putting her elbows on the table.

“Yes!” Candace said, frowning now and leaning back in the chair.  Her toes on the floor, she began to bounce her right heal, her leg jumping.  “So what are you saying?”

Ariadne’s gaze was even.  “I just don’t think a Christian would question someone hearing from God, that’s all.”

Candace looked offended, sputtered for a moment, but made no comment.  After a pause, Hal said, his voice gentle.  “Candace, have you ever heard from God?”

At first she looked defiant, her face growing red.  But gradually, the anger left her eyes and she looked more sad, perhaps even a little defeated.

“Well,” she said, “I thought so.  I’ve only been a Christian for three years.  I’d been working as a radio journalist, then some smaller television stations in Michigan where I’d gone to school. I was getting really frustrated at the thought that I was approaching thirty and getting nowhere in this business, which is, by the way, highly competitive.”  Hal and Ariadne nodded.  “Then a friend invited me to a big church rally thing and I got… well, ‘saved’ I guess.  After that, my friend told me to trust God with my career decisions – to let Him decide for me.  So I asked Him what to do.  I was absolutely certain He told me to pull up stakes, move to DFW, and that He’d make me a success in the field.”

She pushed away from the table, walked over to the coffeepot and poured two mugs of coffee.  Walking back to the table, she sat back down, pushing one mug across the table at Hal.

“Thank you,” he said, picking it up.

Candace nodded.  She blew into the cup, steam flowing off the hot coffee and wafting up into the air.  Candace frowned into the cup.  Hal and Ariadne waited quietly.

“So here I am.  It took a whole year before I even found a job.  I worked as a waitress and a cosmetic counter saleswoman before I got the job at KKTV.  It’s not a major station.  I’ve been there two years and still report on tree cutting and road closures and school board meetings.  I’m thirty-one.  My chances of getting an anchor position are swiftly declining with each year.”  She stopped, chuckling and waving her hand, gesturing to the room around her.  “And now!” she said, her voice rising, “Now I don’t know what is happening!”

She took a large swallow of coffee, wincing as the hot liquid burned her throat.  “So my one shot at hearing from God was not so successful.  I don’t try that anymore.”

There was silence for several moments, interrupted only by the sipping of hot drinks.  

“Candace?” It was Ariadne.  “Do you believe Jesus died for you?”

Candace looked defeated when she looked over at Ariadne.  “Yes,” she sighed, nodding.  “I do.”

“And you recognize that that is your only way into heaven?  By believing on Him and accepting that gift?”

Candace sighed again.  “Yes,” she repeated, nodding again.  “Yes, I do.”

Ariadne looked over at Hal.

“Well then,” he said, “you are certainly ‘saved.’” He accented the word as she had.  Sighing himself, he leaned forward, sliding his forearms onto the tabletop.  “Sometimes,” he said, knowing he sounded like a father, “sometimes it just takes awhile for things to ‘kick in,’ you know?  I mean, it’s quite possible God did tell you to come to the Dallas, Ft. Worth area, and he did give you the job at your station.  I mean, we can’t see the whole plan all the time, you know?”

Hal frowned and sighed.  He was doing this all wrong.  “You remember,” he said, “those pictures we used to look at in magazines as kids?  They were close-up shots of an ordinary item, magnified hundreds of times, and we were supposed to figure out what they were?”

Ariadne’s face lit up.  “Yes!  I remember those!  I hardly ever got them right, but was so fascinated with the pictures!”

Hal nodded, looked back at Candace who was eying him almost suspiciously.  “That little section or orange or whatever,” he said, “doesn’t look like much of anything when you’re that close to it.  You can’t even tell what it is for sure.  But then they showed you a picture of the same item from a ways off and suddenly it made sense.  It was a slice of orange and it was clear as a bell.”

Candace raised one eyebrow.  Hal cleared his throat and tried to get to the point.  “That’s like us and God,” he said.  “We’re here right up close to the bit of orange and can’t hardly tell what it is.  But God is back where it’s clear and makes sense – He can see the whole picture.  So we just ask Him and trust that even though it doesn’t look like much here, He can see it all and as long as we follow His voice, it will all. . . well. . . work out.  Make sense.  You know?”  Hal finished weakly, glancing at Ariadne who smiled encouragingly.

After a long silence during which Candace kept a steady gaze on Hal, she finally said, “So God told me to come to Dallas so I would get blown out of a store during a tornado and land in some mystery place with three strangers and no apparent way of getting back to actually complete anything.”

Hal’s face reddened and he glanced again at Ariadne.

Ariadne straightened her petite frame and looked directly at Candace across the table from her.  “How sure were you that God was speaking to you back then?” she asked, her voice firmer than they had ever heard it.  “Be honest.  Think hard.  How sure were you?”

Candace looked surprised at first, then thoughtful.  Finally she said, “I was very sure.  Since then, I’ve just figured it was the emotional high I was on following that church experience.”

“Never mind that,” Ariadne said, waving her hand.  “Let’s stick to facts.  You accepted Christ, you heard from God, you acted on what you heard.  That is all you need to know.  You may never know why He directed you the way He did.  But our job is simply to ask, then obey.  You did that.  I suggest you open your ears back up.  He’s likely not done directing you yet.”

Candace looked surprised at Ariadne’s words, or at least her tone, then frowned again and fidgeted in the seat.

“Are you okay?” Hal asked.

“I’ve been sitting too long,” she mumbled.  “My leg is going to sleep.”

“Uh oh!”

They all turned to see Elliott in the doorway, his hair and clothes disheveled, his eyes barely focused.  He was weaving a bit on his feet.

“What?” Hal said.

Elliott pointed at Candace.  When they turned to look at her, she was rubbing her arms and looking frightened. She was also appearing to fade before their eyes.  As Elliott had.   “I feel –“ she began.

“Be prepared!” Elliott yelled as Candace quickly faded from view and disappeared.

Chapter 6

She was sitting in a car, in the driver’s seat.

Well, Candace thought, at least it’s not moving.

Looking around, it appeared she was out in the country somewhere.  The road was barely two lanes, paved a long time ago as evidenced by the potholes and eroded edges.  There were no buildings in sight, just fields and the scrub trees that lined the road.  Candace sighed with relief when she saw the keys were in the ignition.  Doesn’t matter where I am, she thought, I can find my way back to the city.

She turned the key and was rewarded with nothing but a clicking sound. Frowning, she turned it again.  Pumped the gas and turned it again.  Pounded the steering wheel and turned it again.  Only clicks each time.

Candace dropped her head down on the steering wheel.  She didn’t know whether to laugh or cry.  But she hadn’t cried in a very long time.  She settled for a derisive laugh and pounded the steering wheel one more time for good measure.  She was reaching for the handle of the door to get out when she heard a noise.  Looking up into the rearview mirror, she saw an old pickup truck pulling up behind her.  A tall, very thin young man unfolded from the driver’s seat and started toward her door.  Instinct borne of independence and city living told her to flee, but there was nowhere to go.  Sighing, Candace cranked down the window as the man reached the side of the car.

“Hey,” he said.  He was very young, probably a teenager.  He was wearing worn, but not fashionably worn, jeans and a faded blue t-shirt.  His expression was pleasant, but tired as he brushed his unruly black hair out of his eyes.  “You need help?”

Candace sucked in her lower lip.  If he could get the car working, she could get to the city and the station.  Did she have a story to tell them….

“Yes,” she finally said.  “My, um, car doesn’t seem to want to start.”

The boy frowned and nodded.  “You mind trying it so I can hear?”

Candace turned the key and heard the now-familiar click.

The boy nodded again, his face serious.  “You mind popping the hood?  Might be your battery is dead.”

Grateful that the boy was heading back to his car and therefore didn’t see her fumbling around in the unfamiliar car for a hood release, Candace finally found it and pulled, the hood popping up a few inches.  She looked in the rearview mirror to see the boy starting his truck and pulling forward, turning toward the side of the road.  His front tires dropped off the broken pavement, and she could hear him gunning his engine to pull them back up when he went into reverse.  Soon he had his truck in front of and facing her, its hood up also.  Candace stepped out of the car as he walked over and easily lifted the hood of her car.  Leaning in, he did a quick check, then walked back to his truck, pulling jumper cables from behind the driver’s seat.  Deftly hooking them up on each vehicle, he looked up at her and nodded.

Candace smiled.

“You mind trying to start it now?” he asked.

Embarrassed, Candace exclaimed, “Oh! Of course!” and sat back down in the car.  Crossing her fingers, she turned the key in the ignition, pressing down on the gas pedal for added luck.  Nothing.

“Okay!” she heard the boy call, and she turned off the key.

“Not the battery,” he said as she stepped around the front of the car again.  “Probably your starter motor.  Maybe the alternator.”  He stood and frowned up at the sky, brushing his hair back again.  “Going to storm soon.”  He looked over at Candace who was peering up at the gathering storm clouds.

“Is there someone you can call?”

“What day is this?” she asked.

“Ma’am?”

“Day,” she said simply, probably too harshly.  “What day is this?”

“Well, it’s Thursday.”

Candace’s face registered shock.  “Thursday? What’s the date?”

He told her.  It was the day of the storm.

“Ma’am, is there someone you can--?”

“No,” Candace practically barked.  “I’ve lost my cell phone.  Can I use yours?”

The boy just looked at her.  “I don’t have a cell phone.”

“Is there a phone at your house?  Can you drive me into town?  Where are we, exactly?”

The boy’s expression was becoming more suspicious by the question.  He blinked slowly and frowned at her, stretching up to his full height, which was several inches past six feet, she was sure.  

“How exactly did you get out here, ma’am?” he asked.  “Not too many folks just accidently end up in these parts.”

“Long story,” Candace said dismissively, looking around them.  There was nothing to be seen.  “How far are we from the city?”

The boy cocked his head at her.  His eyes seemed very tired and still suspicious, but now also slightly amused.  “Well now, if you mean Clawson, we’re not but about five miles from there.  But if you mean a big city, the closest one is Dallas and that’s about an hour and a half away.

“And no,” he added before she could say more, “I can’t drive you into either because I have to get home.  And no, there’s no phone there either.  But if you let me tow your car I can try to fix it and get you on your way. . . to wherever you were going.”

Candace sucked in her lower lip again and narrowed her eyes at the boy whose expression didn’t change.  Reluctantly accepting the fact that she didn’t have much of a choice, Candace nodded and waved her hand at the car in a gesture of acceptance.  The boy stood for just a moment more looking at her, then turned to his truck.  When he came back, he was carrying a heavy chain.  He stopped in front of her car, the chain draping over his hands.  Candace noticed his hands were stained almost black.

“This may not be very good for your bumper,” he said.

Candace shrugged and gestured again, giving him the go-ahead.

Twenty minutes later, she was sitting in the cab of his pickup, her arms crossed, the bouncing of her legs having more to do with nerves than with the condition of the road.  Within a few minutes, she could see a large farmhouse ahead.  It somewhat resembled the yellow house from wherever it was she had come from, only a little larger, a lot older, and in bad need of a paint job.

The boy pulled to a very slow stop in front of the house.  Just as he stopped the truck, the door to the house opened and a young woman, girl really, stepped out onto the porch, a toddler in her left arm straddling her hip.  She looked worried.  When Candace dropped out of the truck and walked around the front of it, the girl was talking quickly as the boy undid the chain attaching the car to his truck.

“. . . late, Jeremy, if I don’t leave in the next two minutes.  Who’s this?”

Candace looked up and realized the girl was talking about her.  The boy, Jeremy, looked up from the chain at Candace and frowned.  “Um….”

“Candace Richards,” she said, just stopping herself from adding, “KKTV news.”  “Your, um,” she suddenly realized she had no idea what the relationship was, “Jeremy here just rescued me and my dead car from the road.

The girl didn’t frown exactly, but neither did she smile.  Looking briefly at Jeremy who began busying himself with the chain again, she said, “I’m Penny, Jeremy’s sister. Jeremy, will I still be able to take the truck?”

Jeremy was pulling the chain from around the bumper of the car.  Nodding, he said, “Of course.  I’ll fix her car and she’ll be on her way.  She doesn’t have a phone to call for help.”

Penny looked concerned.  “But what about--?”

Jeremy was already lifting a hand.  “I’ll take care of them.  Don’t worry.  Just go to work.”

Penny frowned, looking from her brother to Candace.  Finally, she sighed, shifted the toddler in her arms, and said, “Okay.  Mandy didn’t sleep well again last night, so she’ll be cranky.  Richie is eating breakfast.  Joy has already eaten and is getting dressed.  It looks like rain, so make sure they take their slickers.”

Jeremy nodded, reaching into the bed of the truck to retrieve what looked to Candace like an old tin lunchbox.  Putting that on the ground, he reached in and pushed another, very large, metal box to the end of the truck’s bed.  Dropping the back, he lifted the big box out and, walking obviously with effort, set it on the porch.  Reaching up, he took the toddler, Mandy, Candace supposed, from Penny, then walked back to the truck to put the tailgate up.  Penny was already climbing into the cab of the truck.  Jeremy walked over and leaned in so Penny could kiss Mandy.  

“Be good,” she said, cupping the child’s chin briefly and smiling at her.  Looking up at Jeremy, she said, “See you at five.”  With a brief, almost suspicious, glance at Candace, she pulled the truck door closed, started it up, and pulled forward, circling around to pull back out the way they had come in.

Jeremy stood watching her for a moment.  Candace was sure she heard him sigh.  Then he smiled at Mandy, bouncing her slightly in his arms.

“Mandypoo!” he said playfully, causing the little girl to giggle.  “Let’s go check on the beatniks.”  Smiling slightly at Candace, he tipped his head slightly, encouraging her to follow him into the house.  Raising her eyebrows and looking quickly around, Candace followed, wondering not what she had gotten herself into, but rather what she had just been thrust into.

The inside of the house was just as old looking, just as in need of paint, but Candace was surprised by the neatness of it.  The furniture was aged, most covered with towels, or old sheets or blankets, but they were all neatly covered, the edges and corners crisp.  There were some old photos on the walls and a couple vintage landscape pictures, but most of the wall art were children’s drawings of various stages of maturity – from blob-looking fingerpaintings to a group stick-people portrait to a fairly detailed watercolor of the house she was in with five people on the porch or playing in the yard.  The pictures lit up the otherwise almost colorless room.  

Jeremy walked to the stairs at the back of the room and called up, “Joy!  Bus in fifteen! You ready?”

Candace heard a small female voice call back, “Yep! Coming!”

Jeremy, who hadn’t stopped, continued walking to the left, through a large arched doorway, into the kitchen.  Candace followed, more out of curiosity than anything.  A young boy of no more than eleven, was sitting at the table, hunched over a bowl of half-eaten cereal.  He was a miniature version of Jeremy and only glanced briefly up at him as he walked into the room.  Walking over to the refrigerator, Jeremy pulled out a container of orange juice and took it to the counter.  Still balancing Mandy in his arm, he adroitly pulled a glass and a plastic kids’ cup out of the cupboard, filled them both with juice, popped a lid on the kids cup, then handed it to Mandy who immediately started drinking.  Jeremy sat her down and she ambled off back toward the living room, glancing up shyly at Candace as she passed her.

Jeremy sat down at the table with his glass of juice and looked at the boy.  Candace felt like an invisible spy as she watched the two who had not yet spoken to each other.  When they did start speaking, the tones were so low, she found herself actually leaning in to hear them.

“How you doing?” Jeremy said.

The boy didn’t look up, but nodded, put another spoonful of cereal into his mouth, chewed slowly.

“You get your math homework done?”

Another nod.

“Spell government for me.”

“G-o-v-e-r-n-m-e-n-t.”

“Excellent.  You’ll ace that test today.”

The boy’s face flushed almost imperceptively.

Candace didn’t realize the boy had spoken again until Jeremy answered.  Glancing back at Candace, he said to the boy.  “Her name is Ms. Richards.  Ms. Richards, this is my brother, Richard.  Richie.”

“Candace, please,” Candace said.  Neither of them looked at her.  Jeremy was continuing to talk to his brother in a low voice.

“Her car broke down and she didn’t have a phone to call anyone, so I’m going to fix her car.  I wish it was Saturday and you could help me.  Not sure how I can do it without your help.”

The almost-not-there blush returned.

The little girl’s eyes were a vivid blue.  They almost looked like faceted jewels in her creamy face, sweetly rounded, her cheeks boasting a natural blush.  And, unlike the rest of her family, she was smiling and appeared thrilled to see Candace.  Candace found herself thinking that if she hadn’t been born with the name “Joy,” she could certainly see why she was called that now.

“Hi!” the little girl said, looking brightly up at her.  “My name is Joy! Who are you?”

Glancing briefly at Jeremy who seemed barely to notice, Candace smiled down at the girl.  “My name is Candace,” she said.  “Nice to meet you.”  She found herself holding out her hand.

The little girl looked delighted and took it in a surprisingly firm handshake.  Giggling, she said, “Are you going to stay here?”

Jeremy was standing now from the table, running his hand over Richie’s head, smoothing a cowlick threatening to pop up.  “No, Joy, sorry.  School bus is on the way.  You have your bag?”

Jeremy stood for a moment watching it go.  Candace was sure she heard him sigh again.  

Looking over at Candace, he said, “I promise I’ll get right to your car.  I’m sorry about the delay.  But before I can do anything else, I’ve got at least three story books and a good round of bucking bronco I need to take care of.  There’s tea bags or juice in the kitchen.  And help yourself to any other breakfast you can find.”

Candace watched as Jeremy put Mandy down on the porch, tickling her briefly.  “Where’s today’s story?” he asked her.  Her little face lit up and, still clutching her juice cup, she took off into the house, her big brother right on her heals.

Candace looked around again, her eyes settling on the dead car in the yard, then lifting to the darkening sky.

Oddly, she found herself thinking as she headed back into the house, He forgot their slickers.

Chapter 7

        Elliott’s door was open, so Hal, hearing the quiet sounds of guitar, stopped in the doorway, leaning against the doorframe.  Elliott was sitting in a chair facing the window, strumming lightly.  Hal thought he heard quiet singing.  Interloper guilt warming his face, Hal shifted slightly and softly cleared his throat.  Elliott looked around and smiled.  

“Hey,” he said, tipping his head in invitation.  “Come on in.”

Hal grinned and stepped into the room, looking appreciatively at the instruments and equipment.  “You have a real guitar in here?”

Elliott’s eyebrows went up.  “A ‘real’ guitar?”

“Yeah,” Hal said, continuing to look around.  Spotting what he was looking for, he walked over to the acoustic guitar leaning against its stand.  He gestured toward it and raised his eyebrows questioningly at Elliott who nodded permission.  Picking up the guitar, Hal said, “Right!  A real guitar – no knobs, plugs, or wires.”

“Ah!” Elliott said, standing slightly and using his foot to move his chair so it faced the room.  Sitting again so he was facing Hal who was positioning himself on the futon, he said, “So you’re the anti-techi.”

Hal nodded thoughtfully.  “I suppose I am.”  He began quietly strumming the guitar, his ear tipped down toward the neck of the guitar, listening for proper tuning.  It was perfect.

Elliott, too, was still strumming the electric guitar in his arms, automatically shifting to Hal’s key of G strumming.  Soon the two were in synch, Hal strumming the chords and Elliott picking out a random tune.  

Without stopping, Elliott eventually said, “So what is it you do – did – for a living?”

Humor in his eyes, Hal looked to his right at Elliott.  “I was a computer tech support director for a school district.”

Elliott’s fingers froze over the guitar strings.  “I’m sorry – what?”

Hal chuckled and nodded, continuing to strum the guitar, shifting to a new key which Elliott easily and quickly began to play in also.  “That’s right.”

Elliott’s head tipped, amusement brightening his eyes.  “So,” he said, dragging out the word, “there’s a story there?”

Hal chuckled again.  “I live and work in the world of technology,” he said.  “I watch kids with ear buds almost permanently in place, texting continuously, while watching television or playing video games.  Yet with all that input, if I speak to them they can’t utter a decent ‘how do you do’.  I spent a day at one of the high schools in my district installing new equipment and do you know not one student made eye contact with me?  Not one.”

He strummed a few moments in silence.  “I just don’t think all the technology is helping us as a society, that’s all.”

Elliott continued to pick out a tune in Hal’s chosen key.  “So,” he said, drawing it out this time as well, “must be a pretty frustrating job…”

Hal nodded, his brow knitting as he changed the rhythm of his strumming.  “Very.  Just not much else I can do.  And I have a wife and two kids…. “  Hal’s face reddened and Elliott looked down at his guitar, allowing the man his moment.

“Are you two hungry?”  It was Ariadne at the door.

Neither man stopped playing, but Hal slid down to the end of the futon closest to Elliott and nodded toward the other end.  “Come join us,” he said.

Ariadne smiled a small, grateful smile, and sat gently on the end of the futon.  “I didn’t know you played, too,” she said to Hal.

Grinning, he said, “Not much and not well.”

The two musicians continued their impromptu session, Ariadne looking almost wistfully out the window.

“Do you think she’s coming back?” Before the question left her lips, she had thought she was going to say, “Do you think she’s okay?”

Hal looked up.  “She’s been gone quite a while.”

“She’s been gone a lot longer than me, huh?” Elliott said.

Ariadne frowned slightly.  “No, you’d come back by now, but it’s not that much longer.  It’s really hard to gauge time here, though.”

Elliott stopped playing the guitar and frowned at Ariadne, then looked also at Hal.  “Seriously?  I was gone this long?”

Hal nodded.  “Pretty close, I’d say.”

Elliott sat up, his face puzzled.  “No kidding.  That’s weird.”

“Why?” Ariadne tipped her head at him.

“Well, I didn’t look at a clock or anything, but I told you everything that happened while I was there.  I couldn’t have been there an hour even.  But it sure seems like a whole lot more time than that has gone by since Candace, um, left.”

The two on the futon looked at each other.  “It does,” Hal agreed.

“Whoa,” Elliott said, shaking his head as if to rid it of the idea.  “That whole time warp, time travel thing really messes with my head.  I love science fiction, but that stuff’ll get to me.  Throw in the whole alternate universe thing like we have here and I’m scratching my head and jumping at noises for a week.”

Hal chuckled and leaned back on the futon, the guitar resting across his legs.  “It is strange,” he agreed.  “It was a short time there, but longer here.”

“Or so it seemed,” Ariadne corrected.

“Or so it seemed,” he allowed.  

“And where,” Elliott chimed in, his eyes wide and a finger up in the air, “was ‘there’?”

Hal stifled a laugh.  “Meaning?”

Elliott looked surprised at Hal’s confusion.  “Where was I?  Was it the world we left?  Was it an alternate world?  Was it another place here in this – world, or whatever it is?  Was I,” he added, his excitement building and his voice getting louder, “in an alternate reality?  Or!” he practically shouted, straightening up as a new idea apparently occurred to him.  “Or is this the alternate reality?”

Ariadne, comfortable in loose-fitting jeans and an oversized sweater, crossed her legs up under her.  Facing Hal and Elliott, her elbows on her knees, she said, “I truly doubt we’ll ever know the answers to any of those questions.  Maybe what we should be thinking about is why.”

“’Why?’” Elliott said, leaning forward slightly and wrapping his arms around the guitar.  “Why what?”

“Why did you go there?” she said.  “Why that place?  Why those people?  Was there something you were supposed to do or learn?”

Elliott cocked his head again, frowning.  “I told you.  I believe I was there to play a song and I didn’t have one ready.  I’m hoping I’ll get another chance.  That’s why I’ve been working so hard – so I will be.  Ready.”

“But,” Ariadne said kindly, “why, Elliott?  I mean, this seems much bigger than a song, you know?  Not that your music isn’t important,” she added quickly.

Elliott looked thoughtful.  Clearing his throat and looking suddenly very awkward, he said, not looking directly at either of them, “I haven’t been a Christian for very long.  A buddy of mine gave me some cds to listen to.  I loved the sound of one in particular and kept playing it.  I was mostly listening for the sound.  I’ve never been much of a lyrics guy.  But on about the third playing, I began to hear the words, and then I couldn’t not listen, you know what I mean?  It was all about ‘going to the cross,’ and ‘what You did for me,’ and ‘thank You for loving me.’”

Elliott turned slight in the chair and looked out the window.  “I didn’t get it.  But I listened, and then I started asking questions.  That, of course, is just what my buddy was hoping I’d do.”  Elliott chuckled lightly.  

“I read in the Bible that God is like everywhere, that He leaves his signature on all things in nature so that everyone can get to know Him.  Well, it’s music where I see Him best.  That’s I guess why the Psalms are my favorite part of the Bible.  Somehow when music is involved -- when I’m writing, or playing, or just listening to it -- He seems to be clearer to me.  You know?”

He turned and looked at Hal and Ariadne who were both nodding thoughtfully.

“So,” Elliott continued, “it doesn’t surprise me that my whole. . . otherworldly encounter, or whatever. . . had something to do with music.  I just knew when I was there that the reason for me being there was that chance to go to the stage and play.”  Elliott stopped, looked down at the guitar still in his hands.  “And then I blew it.”

Ariadne and Hal looked at each other.  It was Ariadne who spoke first.

“You did not blow it!  I mean, what a strange situation!  Give yourself a break, Elliott.  Really.”  She twisted her mouth in frustration at her inability to think of anything more encouraging.

“I agree,” Hal spoke up, sensing her frustration.  “Man, how could you have been ready for that?  And look at you now!  You’re making preparations for the next time.”

“If there is a next time,” Elliott added.

Hal frowned, then nodded in agreement.  “Okay, if there is a next time.  But that’s all you can do, El.”

Elliott nodded almost reluctantly.  Hoisting the guitar up in front of him, he began again to strum at the strings.  “That’s why I’m going to be ready,” he said.  “It’ll be the best song I’ve ever written.”

Ariadne drew her knees up under her chin, wrapping her arms around her legs.  “I wonder what lesson Candace is learning,” she said, her voice small.

Candace stared at Jeremy, still cradling the finally-sleeping Mandy in his arms.  Penny called that one, Candace thought wryly.  Mandy may not have slept well last night, but that child just took ‘cranky’ to a whole new level.  

The past hour had been a whirlwind of terror, in Candace’s eyes.  It started calmly enough with Jeremy reading a sweet little rhyming story picture book to his sister.  Mandy’s eyes had begun to droop and her head to loll back a bit on her big brother’s shoulder.  Apparently that portent to sleep was enough to send red alert signals through her toddler brain and she found an adrenaline source they didn’t see coming.  From that point, her only goal was to stay awake and her arch enemy was anyone who thought differently.  

Story books were of no use after that.  The bucking-bronco ride across the living room became alarmingly violent, proffered treats and sippy cups turned into missiles and the decibel level of her little girl screams caused a ringing in Candace’s ears that lasted for thirty minutes after the last scream echoed through the house.

Finally, Jeremy, with another frantic apologetic look at Candace, expertly wrapped his arms around the child, cradling her to him, but in such a way that she could barely move, sat on the couch, and sang “Jesus Loves Me” while rocking back and forth against the cushions almost violently at first, the song sounding like it was being barked through clenched teeth.  Candace must have looked horrified at what appeared to be almost abuse, because Jeremy, Mandy still screaming in his arms, sang, as if they were previously unheard lyrics to the song:

“This will help her go to sleep,

Will you hand me ‘pacie’ please?”

Candace, taking a second to realize what she had just heard, startled into action, bending to retrieve the previously-thrown pacifier from the floor.

“Do you want me to go wash it off?”

Jeremy, still singing, shook his head and opened his mouth.  Candace raised her eyebrows.  The sound coming out as a gargled hum now, he nodded again almost desperately.  Finally, Candace put the pacifier in Jeremy’s open mouth, continuing to stare dumbfounded at him.  When he nodded again, still humming, Mandy still screaming, she didn’t know what to do at first.  Jeremy’s humming took on a more frustrated tone and he looked pointedly, and cross-eyed, down at the pacifier.  

“Oh!” Candace said, pulling it out of his mouth.  

Continuing his almost violent rocking, Jeremy sang,

“Fastest way to clean it off.

Will you put it in her mouth?”

Candace popped the pacifier into the squalling mouth, Jeremy deftly twisting the hand closest to the child’s mouth to hold it in with the tips of his fingers.  Candace could tell she was trying to push it back out with her tongue.

Then, relatively quickly, the screaming plugged, Jeremy’s song became more quiet, the rocking more gentle, and Mandy finally drifted off to sleep.

Candace stared at the duo now.  Jeremy had looked tired before.  Near death would better describe his current state.

After a few moments of silence, he whispered, “I’ll go lay her down –“

Before he could finish, Candace held up both hands, her face shocked.  “Don’t you dare!” she practically hissed.  “And risk starting that up again?”

Jeremy twisted his mouth in reluctant agreement and settled back some on the couch.  Mandy sighed and snuggled up to his chest but didn’t wake up.  

“But I need to fix your car,” he said, his voice no longer a whisper, but quiet.

“My car can wait,” Candace ordered, thinking, If that starts up again, I’ll walk out of here.  Sighing, she said, “How about I get us something to drink.  You need food or something?”

Jeremy smiled shyly, apology constantly present in his eyes.  Nodding reluctantly, he said, “That would be great.  I’m starving.  I know there’s bread out there.  Probably bologna in the fridge.  Peanut butter in the cabinet.  Nothing too fancy, I’m afraid.”

Without saying anything, Candace went into the kitchen to prepare sandwiches.  Like the rest of the house, the kitchen wasn’t fancy, or particularly well stocked, but it was neat, the food in the refrigerator carefully packaged, the cabinets nicely organized.  It was obvious that nothing went to waste in this house.  A few minutes later, Jeremy and Candace were munching on bologna sandwiches and drinking iced tea.

Candace looked closely at Jeremy.  The reporter in her had filled up on questions throughout her morning in the home.

“Jeremy,” she said as gently as possible, “where are your parents?”

It was obvious to the reporter side of Candace that she had just asked a question the boy was not in the habit of answering.  She saw his personal privacy fence quickly rise in his eyes.  But then he looked down at his sleeping sister and sighed.  Candace assumed he saw her as low-risk when he began to answer.

“They been gone awhile,” he said.  “My Dad died from wounds he got in Iraq.  My Mom. . . Well, I’m not sure where she is.  She just had all she could take, I guess.”

In her training as an interviewer, Candace had learned many tricks to keep her subjects talking.  The goal was to get them so relaxed, or so rattled in some cases, that they would forget she was a reporter and tell her more than they had anticipated saying.  She kept her face sympathetic without being condescending, interested without being demanding.

“I’m sorry,” she said, her voice adding an ellipsis to the end of it, knowing he would continue.  

He did.

Their father had been a sergeant in the Army, deployed to Iraq at the beginning of the War.  He’d disappeared from his platoon and was later charged with being A.W.O.L. and dishonorably discharged.  Injured during his time away from his group, he required more medical care once he got home.  Due to the discharge, however, he was no longer covered under military medical.  

“It was a nightmare,” Jeremy said, taking a swallow of his tea.  “My Mom’s job had no medical benefits.  She was expecting Mandy here.  They were denied for public assistance.  My Dad just got weaker and weaker.  Until I guess his heart just gave out.”

Jeremy glanced over at her, then back at the glass in his right hand, then at his sleeping sister still cradled in his other arm.

Candace’s news instinct was tweaked.  “What?” she asked, encouragingly yet gently.

“That’s what we told the family,” Jeremy said very quietly, as if the house itself wasn’t supposed to hear the words.

“What do you mean?”

Jeremy cleared his throat, wouldn’t look up.  “We – my Mom and me – we told the family that his heart just gave out.  Really, he had to stop taking a couple of his medications because they couldn’t get them anymore.  That’s really what killed him.”

Candace’s brow furrowed deeply as she watched Jeremy’s face.  “And your Mom?” she prompted in a quiet voice.

Jeremy cleared his throat again, stared down at Mandy.  “Dad died just after Mandy was born.  Mom left two months later.  The whole thing broke her.  She never was all that strong.  It was Dad that held us all together.  We hated when he was deployed.  With him gone, she just couldn’t do it anymore.  We got up one morning and she was gone.  She’s never come back.”

Candace’s mouth was uncharacteristically open in shock.

“How did you keep the house?”

“It was my grandparents’.  Paid for.  Between me and Penny, we save enough to pay the taxes every year.”

“Jeremy, how old are you?”

Jeremy grinned over at her.  “I’m nineteen.  Penny is seventeen.  We’ve got a pretty good system going here.  We’re both going to get our G.E.D.s one day.  The only one I really worry about is Richie.  He was closest to Mom and old enough to understand what was going on…”

Jeremy stopped, his face turning red.  Candace recognized the signs of someone who just realized he had said too much.   Doing some quick damage control, she made sure her voice held only admiration and no condemnation when she said, “So you’re doing all this yourselves?”

Nodding, Jeremy smiled down at his sleeping sister.  “Not always so great a job,” he said, his eyes smiling at Candace with remembrance of the past hour.  “But we do okay.”  He tipped his head back on the couch, his eyes closed.

Interview over, Candace thought, smiling to herself.  Crossing her legs, the top leg bouncing, Candace frowned.  This, she thought, is a story.  Is that why I’m here?  For the story?  Elliott had said it was all about being prepared.  He had been given the opportunity to sing one of his songs and hadn’t been ready.  Here she was being given the works for a good story.  Candace’s mind went to the lead line, the angle of the story.  As her eyes scanned the room, the print rolled through her head of her description of the place.  She pictured herself back here with a cameraman, saw the drawings on the wall as they would appear in the lens.  The help would come pouring in for these kids and she would be in the spotlight.  She envisioned positioning the camera to show her also – maybe with little Joy on her lap!  Candace smiled and nodded.

“Funny thing was,” Jeremy said, causing Candace to snap out of her reverie and look over at him.  He was looking up at the ceiling.  “Funny thing was, my Dad never was A.W.O.L.  He’d been injured and when he came around, his squad was gone.  An Iraqi family took him in to help him.  When he could travel, he found his way back to his men.  They had moved him from M.I.A. to A.W.O.L. and discharged him.”

Candace stared, thunderstruck.  In one statement, she went from human interest feature to investigative news.  And one step closer to that anchor desk.

“Jeremy,” she said, “I need some paper and a pen.”

Chapter 8

By some miracle, Mandy stayed asleep and Jeremy stayed awake.  Following his instructions, Candace had located some paper and scrounged up three stubby pencils.  Using her own brand of shorthand developed over the years during interviews, Candace jotted notes as Jeremy talked again, telling his father’s story in as much detail as he knew.

Jeremy’s father, Staff Sergeant Clarence “Chuck” Taylor, had been moving with his platoon through a remote area when they were suddenly fired upon from the hills.  The men had scattered, taking cover among rocks and trees as best they could, returning fire, but blindly.  Chuck didn’t know how long the battle raged because he was hit in the first round and was flung down an embankment, his body coming to rest among some scrub bushes, hiding him from sight.  He surmised later that his platoon must have scattered and pulled out pretty quickly, choosing to pull back and regroup rather than engage since the enemy was hidden.  Otherwise they would have done a thorough search and rescue and would likely have found him.  He’d seen skirmishes like that which were massive confusion.  Only later could you do a headcount and hope everyone was there.  When they discovered Sgt. Taylor missing, they would have sent a smaller squad to find him.   When they didn’t, he was listed as missing in action.

In the meantime, Sgt. Taylor woke up in the small crude home of an Iraqi family, living outside the city in a remote area, trying to live as if their country wasn’t being ravaged by war and death.  Fluent in English, they had explained that they were opposed to their government, and were trying to keep a low profile out in the desert, praying to Allah that the war would not find them.  One of the children, out playing, brought the war home to them when he found the wounded and unconscious American soldier.  The family brought him home, the parents expertly tending his wounds, and nursing him through several days of fever and delirium.  

It was two weeks before Chuck was coherent enough to realize that he was lost from his battalion, but still too weak to do anything about it.  His radio was useless, damaged in the fall, and the family had no means of communication.  It was too risky to send anyone out in search of his men, and the area where they lived was so remote, the chances of any troops coming that way were almost nil.  So Sgt. Taylor did the only thing he could do:  gain his strength as quickly as possible to get back.

That would prove to be more difficult than expected.  The bullets which had riddled his body had gone right through, but internal damage was worse than first thought.  He was very weak, very sick, his body not functioning properly in many ways.  To make matters even worse, his left leg was almost useless.  Walking was, at first, impossible, only improving to barely possible.  

Desperation set in.  Chuck knew he had to find his way back to his men.  If they hadn’t found him, they would have declared him missing, presumed dead, and would have informed his family.  He also knew he had to get back because he was no longer improving, was indeed getting worse, and he knew he would surely die in this place.  

Finally, with tremendous gratitude to the family, giving them, against their protests, all the money he carried on him, Sgt. Taylor accepted a leather skin of water and a heavy branch to use as a crutch, and went out in search of his men.  It took two full days of walking before he finally saw an approaching U.S. motorcade.  By then he was so weak, he was closer to dead than alive.  He collapsed in front of the vehicles and woke up several days later in a military hospital in Germany.  But that was when his nightmare really began.

His first conscious knowledge was a military doctor informing him that he was being transferred home, Texas, to a civilian hospital there and that the Army’s responsibility stopped there.  Totally confused, too sick to fully understand what was happening, it wasn’t until he was transferred home that Sgt. Taylor found out he had been dishonorably discharged due to being absent without leave.  Away from his platoon, unable to connect with his commanding officers, Chuck never knew with certainty what had happened.  His best guess was someone, an Army scout perhaps, had seen him at the Iraqi home and reported him there and not under duress.  That would be enough to have him declared A.W.O.L.  No longer having his military resources, and unable to afford civilian help, Chuck didn’t know how to fight the system that was denying he and his family the benefits due him.  Also unable to afford the hospital bills, he checked himself out of the hospital.

He survived only a few more months.  Unable to fight the war at home that would get his family public assistance and him the insurance he needed for the necessary medical care, Staff Sergeant Clarence Taylor died disillusioned, angry, and very frightened for his family.  His wife, never strong, and unable to cope, left a short time later, leaving their five children to fend for themselves.

Candace sat back in the chair, flipping through the papers, frowning at her notes and putting the story together in her head.  This family had a major lawsuit on their hands.  Candace thought through what they would need to investigate, what documents they would need, in order to prove the case.  She knew just which sources she would call.  The story would start with a general attention-getter.  Sympathy for the family would be strong, public outcry loud.  They would read some of the emails from the public on the news throughout the first week.  Updates toward the end of the week would keep interest piqued.  She would be able to demand desk time to make the reports herself after the first two films aired.  That would demonstrate to the producers and station owners that she had the type of studio camera presence to carry the full news and not just individual stories.  She would be a shoo-in for the news anchor spot when it formally opened.

Candace smiled and sighed contentedly.

She looked up when she saw Jeremy moving.  “What are you doing?”  

“I have to lay her down,” he said quietly.  “We’ve got her sleeping about six hours at night and about five during the day so Penny and I can both get some sleep.  If I don’t lay her down, she’ll wake up early and Penny will have a devil of a time with her tonight.”

Guilt burned her face as Candace watched Jeremy, his eyes red-rimmed with fatigue, carry Mandy out of the room and up the stairs.  She hadn’t thought about the fact that he had just come off a night shift and this was his time to sleep.  And he still had to fix her car.

Candace frowned.  Fix the car, then what?  Thinking of Hal, Elliott, and Ariadne, Candace wondered if she was free of the yellow house.  Was this her second chance?  Her opportunity of a lifetime?  She chewed on the inside of her lip as she thought about what the whole thing meant.  Was this just a dream, or was she truly being given a chance to write a career-advancing story?  Would she be “allowed” to go back to the city and continue with the story?  Maybe the idea was to keep her out of that ill-fated store when the storm hit.  She looked up as Jeremy came back into the room, a baby monitor in his hand.  He set the monitor on the table in front of the couch, then stood there, his fingers tucked into his jeans pockets.  

“I better go get that car fixed,” he said.  “You mind listening out for Mandy?  If you hear anything through here,” he nodded toward the monitor, “just come holler at me.”

Candace nodded and watched him walk out the door, holding the screen so it didn’t slam behind him.  Frowning, Candace acknowledged the guilt that was plaguing her.  This boy’s only time to sleep was now.  With the story telling she’d made him do, and now the car repair, he wouldn’t get any sleep before he had to go back to work again tonight.  But what choice did she have?  She had to get this story to the station.

Candace walked around the coffee table to sit on the couch, spreading the papers across the table in front of her.  Scanning the pages, she penciled in notations as she thought of connections, angles, subjects of research.   She marked the sections she would do again with Jeremy on film and bracketed the parts she would quote directly on her voice-over.  Furious at the loss of her phone, she marked at the bottom of the pages who she would have to call for verification, counsel, and interviews.  Looking at the stubby pencil lead, she grinned.  It had been a long time since this work was done on paper.  She hadn’t realized she could still do it without her smartphone and netbook.

After several minutes of working, she leaned back on the couch and looked around the room, the metallic sounds of Jeremy working in the yard in the background.  On the wall in front of the couch was a simple row of the children’s school pictures.  The first two were Jeremy and Penny, obviously taken a few years earlier.  The third was Richie, not smiling, his eyes almost haunting in their gaze.  That boy is going to need therapy, Candace thought, and smiled when she thought about the money the family would get as a result of the story.  The next picture was Joy.  Candace, still smiling, shook her head.  That child was special.  A warm feeling began to grow in Candace as she thought about the brighter future the little girl would have now.  There was no picture of Mandy on the school wall, of course, but – as if the child knew – Candace right then heard a little squeaking noise from the monitor.  Holding her breath, she prayed silently that the noise would stop.  He’s got to finish that car, she thought, willing the child to stay asleep.  After a few minutes with no further noise, Candace sighed with relief and looked back at her papers.

Her hand looked funny.  Candace held her hand up in front of her.  It felt tingly, like it was asleep.

“Oh no.”

Bolting up from the couch, Candace looked around desperately.  “No!” she said loudly, then looked apologetically at the baby monitor, holding her breath.  No sound from Mandy.

I can’t leave now!  She thought, looking down at the papers on the table.  I have to do this story!

She held both hands up in front of her.  They looked normal.  Felt normal.  Candace fell back onto the couch in relief.  But it had happened, almost as a warning.  She knew beyond doubt she was about to buzz out of this place.

And go where?  Would she be back home, back at the station, or back in that blasted yellow house with the other three?  Candace frowned, tried to force some clarity into her thoughts.  Elliott had gone and come back.  But he had blown the reason he was there.  She was obviously here for this story, but if she left now, she wouldn’t have a chance to prove herself.  Maybe she’d already done what she was supposed to and now she was going to get to go back to her real life and finish working the story.

Or maybe, she realized slowly, the dawning idea causing her frown to deepen, maybe I was here for another reason.  Maybe, she thought, the words moving lazily through her mind, the comprehension of each word coming slowly and almost painfully, maybe . . . it’s. . . not. . . the story.  Candace looked up again from her position on the couch at the pictures on the wall.  

She’d had a journalism professor in college who taught a section on personal interest pieces.  “Fluff,” Candace and most of her classmates had complained.  The professor looked like the woman on the Betty Crocker cookbook cover and occasionally slipped and referred to her college students as “boys and girls.”  Candace usually did work on her phone or netbook under the table while the woman taught.  But one day when they came into the class, the usually talkative and animated teacher was just standing at the front of the room, the podium in front of her, the screen pulled down over the whiteboard behind her.  She stood silently while the class filed in and took their seats.  Then she looked slowly around the room and said, “Why?”

There was silence and nervous glances around the room, a few embarrassed grins.

“Why,” she repeated, “do we do this job?  What is the point?”

Candace grinned and looked down, wondering if she was witnessing a journalistic breakdown like she saw in a movie once.

“One answer,” the teacher said.  She looked slowly around the room, then, without a word, turned and lifted the screen behind her.  On the board, previously covered by the screen, were two words:

“The People.”

Without looking back up, she turned and walked out of the room.  It was thirty minutes before the students got the idea that it was a two-word lecture that day and class was over.  The woman was back for the next class, as talkative as ever, but she had made her point, and made it dramatically.

The people.  Candace frowned at the pictures on the wall.  Not the story, she thought, the idea causing her to flinch, but the people.

Just then her hand tingled again.

Candace actually growled with frustration as she picked up the least stubby pencil and began scribbling furiously on the pages in front of her – notes of explanation for whoever would get the pages, more detail to define her personal shorthand.  Grumbling to herself as she wrote, Candace tried to think of who would do the best job on the story.  Greg would not be detailed enough, Phil not tough enough.  Still writing, Candace marched through more names, dismissing each one for various reasons.  Stopping suddenly, she looked down at the top paper.  She had just written a name across the top of the page.  Shocked, barely recognizing that she had written it, Candace stared at the name.  It was her competition for the anchor position.  Sighing, Candace laid the pencil down on the stack of papers.  She’ll do the best job, she admitted.  Grinning and shaking her head, she thought, She’ll probably do a better job than I would.

The tingling was getting worse.  Sighing with a new resolve, Candace picked up a blank piece of paper and wrote another note on it, placing it next to the stack of interview notes.  Standing, she ran her tingling hands down her thighs and looked around the room.  She could hear thunder in the distance.  

Maybe, she thought.  Maybe I’ll end up at the station.  But somehow she knew that wasn’t true.

Her feet were beginning to prickle and she was getting a little light-headed.  She had to hurry.

Walking out to the porch, she took the steps carefully.  Jeremy’s head was under the hood of the car.  “Jeremy.”

The boy looked up, stepped around the car, wiping his hands on a dirty rag.  “She wake up?”

It took a moment for Candace to realize what he was asking.  “Oh, no.  Sound asleep.  Listen, I have to go –“

“Sorry,” he said, “I’ll be done in a bit, but I’m having trouble –“

“No,” Candace interrupted, holding up her hand.  “I, uh, my boss is meeting me just up the road.”

Jeremy frowned and tipped his head.

“I totally forgot that he said that he’d meet me up here at, uh,” she looked at her watch, hoped it was the right time, “one o’clock.  He’s going to be a little ticked that I didn’t work on the job he gave me.”  She laughed nervously, squeezed her tingling fingers.  “Anyway, I’ll walk down to meet him, but I have some really important things I need you to hear.  Okay?”

Jeremy was still frowning, but stopped wiping his hands, crossed his arms, and nodded.  

Candace took a breath.  “Okay.  First, I’ve put all my notes together in there from talking to you.  You have a real case here, Jeremy.  I think you and your family stand a very good chance of getting the help that your father earned and deserved.  I’ve written the name of the person to give this to at the top of the paper.  Please don’t hesitate.  She’ll take care of everything.” As she said it, Candace knew in her heart it was true.  She smiled.  “Seriously.  You’re all going to be just fine.”

Thunder sounded and Candace frowned up at the sky.  “Second, do you have a storm cellar here?”

Jeremy raised his eyebrows, but nodded over his right shoulder.  “Yeah, over –“

“Good,” she interrupted.  “When the children get home, get them in that cellar.  It’s going to be a very bad storm.  Take the notes with you.  What time does Penny get home?”

“Four.”

“Good,” Candace said, calculating in her head.  “Good.”  The tingling was moving up her legs.  She had to get out of there before Jeremy saw a change in her.

“I’ve got to go,” she said, and began to walk toward the road.  “Please remember what I said about the notes.” She picked up the pace, talking over her shoulder.  “Please remember to get the family in the storm cellar.”  She was practically running now.  “And there’s one more note in there for you too.  Good luck, Jeremy!”  She was running full-out now, her legs a little shaky.  She thought he called something after her, but she kept going, anxious to be out of sight before she was truly out of sight.

Jeremy Taylor watched the woman running down the drive, his confusion complete.  She hadn’t answered about whether she was coming right back for the car.  Shrugging, he wiped his hands again, dropping the rag on the porch rail.  Strangest day ever, he thought, and walked slowly into the house.  He could probably still get a couple hours sleep.  That would be better than nothing.  He walked over to the coffee table, listening for Mandy in the baby monitor.  Nothing, thank goodness.

Jeremy sat on the couch, weary to his bones.  He looked up at the pictures of his family on the wall, as was his custom before he went to sleep each day on the couch.  Just a visual reminder of what it was all about.  He grinned.  Worth it, he reminded himself, as he did every day.  Before he lay down on the cushion, he looked at the stack of papers on the table.  Frowning in puzzlement as he flipped through them, he was thankful he didn’t have to make sense of the writing.  He thought again about what the woman – Candace? – had said.  Was there really a chance that the family could get some help?  Jeremy shrugged again, but an odd buzz of excitement had started in him when she spoke the words and he knew he would pursue it.

He lay back on the cushions, sleep crowding his brain almost immediately.  Just before he totally nodded off, he remembered her saying something about another note.  Sitting back up, he picked up the single piece of paper next to the stack.  Reading it, he shook his head in wonder, then chuckled lightly.  Strange day, indeed.  He held the note in his hand as he lay back down, asleep almost immediately.

“Jeremy,” the note said, “keep the car.  God bless, Candace.”

Chapter 9

 

“Oh crap.”

She was back in the yellow house, in the room designed for her.  Somehow she had hoped. . . She had come to know that she wasn’t going back to the station, but coming back to the house meant that the ordeal wasn’t over yet.  Now what, she thought glumly.  Grabbing some clean clothes, she went down the hall to the bathroom, locked the door behind her, and started the shower water.  Leaning on the sink with both hands, she stared at her reflection in the mirror.

“Candace Richards, girl reporter.”  She smirked.  “Biggest story of my career and I gave it away.”  Suddenly, Joy’s face came to Candace’s mind, as did the weary and red-eyed face of Jeremy, the teenager working to support his family.  Candace felt a heat in her belly – the kind she got when a story went well, the parts falling into place securely, the sound bites perfect in timing and inflection.  Straightening up, she frowned at herself in the mirror.  “What’s happening to you?” she demanded of her reflection.  Mumbling to herself about getting soft and losing her edge, she turned away from the mirror and toward the shower.

“Oh, okay, so. . . “

Hal and Ariadne looked up from the chairs as Elliott came into the living room.  

“El?” Hal said.

Elliott looked perplexed and nervous, his hands were gesturing, but only partial words were coming out of his mouth.  After a moment, he managed, “Shower’s running, but you two are – that is, we’re not – I mean, well, I’m not…..”

Ariadne stood up suddenly.  “She’s back?”

It took a moment for Hal to grasp what had happened.  Suddenly his face lit up.  “Oh! Candace is in the shower!  Okay.”

Elliott looked relieved to have been understood.

 “She’s been gone awhile,” Hal said.  “I can’t wait to hear what happened to her.”

Ariadne was sitting back down, but frowning.  

“What is it?” Hal asked.

Ariadne twisted her mouth to the side.  “Well, I know this sounds strange, but I was rather hoping she wouldn’t come back.”

Elliott was dropping down on the couch across from them.  “I know what you mean,” he said, stretching his legs out and planting his feet on the glass coffee table.  “I mean I like her and all, but she’s not exactly the life of the party, if you get my meaning.”

Ariadne just stared at him a moment.  Hal stifled a grin.

“I meant,” Ariadne said slowly, “that I was hoping she’d find a way out of here for herself.  That she’d do whatever it was while she was away that would ensure her. . . well, release I suppose.”

Elliott’s face reddened.  “Oh.  Sorry.”

Hal started to speak, but before he could say anything, from behind him he heard, “Honey, I’m home!”

The three stood as Candace came down the steps and into the room.  Uncomfortable silence and embarrassed looks prevailed as Candace, wearing a loose-fitting jogging suit, her hair wet and combed back into a ponytail, walked to the couch and sat down, crossing one leg over the other.  The other three slowly sat down.

“Well.”  Candace grinned at them.  “That was interesting.”

There was a sudden explosion of simultaneous questions from the other three.  Candace laughed and held up one hand.  “Okay,” she said, “I’ll start at the beginning, but I have a question first.  How long was I gone?”

Ariadne was the first to answer.  “A long time!  Much longer than Elliott.  It’s so hard to gauge time here, but we’ve gone through the rest of that day, a night, and most of today.”

Candace’s eyes widened.  “Really!  I think I was there for like five or six hours is all.”  She frowned and looked toward the window, her crossed leg swinging.  “And it was still Thursday, the day of the storm.”  Grinning, Candace added, “I think I saw a movie like this once.”

Hal nodded, also grinning.  “I believe I recall a television series or two about this sort of thing, too.”

“Weird,” Candace agreed.

“The time thing is really freaking me out!” Elliott spouted.  “I mean, Hal was gone outside for a long time by his calculations, but to us here it was like no time had passed.  But Candace and I were gone for relatively short times, but it’s a whole lot longer here. Days seem to go by here, but when we get zapped back into the world, it’s that same day over again.  I don’t get it.  I really don’t get it!”

Hal smiled patiently.  “None of us get it, Elliott,” he said.  He turned to Candace.  “Do you want to tell us about what happened?”

Candace, knowing how anxious they were to hear, was tempted to say “no” just for the comical effect.  Instead, she pulled her feet up under her in uncharacteristic relaxation, and said, “It was the craziest thing…”

For the next several minutes, she told them the story of Jeremy and Penny and the children, of Staff Sergeant Clarence Taylor and an injustice done to an American soldier.  As she told it, she used some of the lines she had planned to use for the broadcast.  She was pleased to see the effect they had on her audience.  She ended with the tingling starting and her having to run.

“But,” Ariadne had been leaning forward in the chair as Candace spoke.  “But, what was the point?  I mean, you got the story, but you can’t report the story, right?  So what was the point?  Why were you there?”

Flashing quickly on her journalism instructor, Candace found herself saying, “The people.”

Hal’s eyebrows went up in surprise.  “What are you saying?”

Candace cleared her throat and slumped a little on the couch, although it was from fatigue rather than from failure.  “I thought I was there to write the All-American story,” she said, her voice softer than they’d heard it before.  “I remembered what you said, Elliott, about being prepared and not failing at the task given.  So I did the best I could to get the information I needed, then made notes about who to call and what to do from there.  I had the perfect story all laid out.”  She looked at each of them, her eyes weary, but content.  “And then I realized I was leaving.  So I had a decision to make.”

Candace smiled, her eyes shining.  She was feeling better than she had in a long, long time.  “I had to decide what the point of it was.  And I had to admit that the point was not for me to tell the story, but simply that the story be told.  The important part,” she said quietly, perhaps more to herself than the others, “was that family.”

There was silence in the room for several minutes.  Finally, Candace sighed and slapped her hands on her thighs.  

“So,” she said brightly, “why the heck am I back here?  I kind of thought I did my good deed, learned a big lesson, and so I’d be buzzed on up and out of here, you know?”

“Actually,” Hal said, “I was thinking that was it exactly.  There’s something for us to learn, and we’re here until we learn it.”

“Or,” Ariadne added, “something for us to do, and we’re given the opportunity to do it.”

There was no malice in Candace when she said to Ariadne, “So you think I didn’t do what I was supposed to?”

Ariadne quickly shook her head.  “Oh no!” she said.  “It seems to me you did just exactly what you were supposed to do.  I just can’t imagine. . . “

There was silence for a moment, then Candace smacked her hands on her legs again.  “Well,” she said, “I’m hungry!  That bologna sandwich was a long while ago by whatever standard of time you choose to use.”  She stood and started toward the kitchen.

“Me too!” Elliott said and followed her.

Standing, Ariadne looked at Hal, “Can I bring you something?”

Hal was still frowning slightly.  “A cup of coffee?”

Ariadne smiled.  “I’ll get Candace to make it,” she said, walking toward the kitchen.

Candace and Elliott were standing in front of the open refrigerator, staring.  Ariadne walked up behind them and cleared her throat.  “Problem?”

“Hungry,” Elliott said, not looking at her.

“Can’t decide,” Candace said, also continuing to stare into the refrigerator.  

Ariadne smiled, waited a moment, then put her two hands between them and pushed them gently apart.  Bending and looking into the fridge, she moved a few things, opened a few drawers, checked compartments on the door.

“Okay,” she said, standing up and rubbing her hands together, “how about cheese omelets, hash browns with onions, and buttered toast?”  When she didn’t get an answer, she turned and looked at the other two.  They were both looking at her with expressions of shocked admiration.

“Can you make that happen?” Elliott asked, a sort of wonder in his voice.

“If you help,” she answered.

Elliott and Candace took a step back.  “Just tell us what to do!” Candace said.

A few moments later, Elliott was peeling potatoes, Candace was shredding cheese, and Ariadne was chopping onions.  

“Wow.”  It was Hal who had just walked into the room.  “Quite an operation you have here.”

Ariadne looked up from the onion.  “Sorry, Hal.  I forgot about the coffee.”

Shaking his head, Hal walked toward the coffee pot.  “No problem!  I’m on it.  Candace?”

“Oh yes!” she said.  “Make enough for me, too.”

In a short time, the meal was done and being piled onto plates.  Candace had wanted to scour the freezer for bacon, but out of deference for Ariadne had resisted the temptation.  Sitting again in the living room, the four ate in silence for some time.  

After a while, Candace said, “Please don’t take this the wrong way, but I was really hoping not to come back here.”

“Actually,” Ariadne said, grinning over at a blushing Elliott, “we were rather hoping not to see you here again.”

There was silence in the room for awhile.  Then Elliott said quietly, almost fearfully, “What if we’re here forever?”

“How could that be?” Candace said.  “I mean, I don’t claim to be a Bible scholar or anything, but this doesn’t fit anything I’ve ever heard about Heaven or the afterlife.”

Ariadne nodded, held a finger up for them to wait while she chewed.  Swallowing, she said, “I thought for a little while that God had nothing to do with this, that it was some sort of cosmic mess-up.  But now I don’t think so.  I don’t know about the rest of you, but I feel Him here.  Actually, I’m feeling His presence more and more.  Maybe it just took getting me out of my routine, but I feel closer to Him somehow.”

Candace nodded thoughtfully.  “Someone guided what I did with Jeremy and his family,” she said.  “I know I wouldn’t have done it that way.”

“I agree,” Elliott said.  “I knew He was showing me my purpose for being in that club.”  He looked over at Hal who was watching the floor.  “Hal? You don’t agree?”

Hal was frowning.  “I can’t disagree,” he said finally.  “You’re just telling how you feel.  Nobody can disagree with your feelings.  But,” he added, still making very little eye contact, “I can’t say that I’ve felt the same thing.”

“Really?” Candace said, her head tipping.  “How do you feel?”

Hal looked up and at her for a long time, Candace holding his gaze patiently.  It seemed he was making a decision and, when he finally spoke, had decided to take the risk.

“Well,” he said, “I don’t feel much of anything where God is concerned.”  There was absolute silence in the room.  “I mean, I am a Christian.  I still pray and go to church.  I just don’t. . . Well, I don’t know.  I just don’t feel anything.  My head knows it’s true and I still believe in my salvation and in the gospel, so to speak.  There’s just no….” He finally trailed off, shrugging.

“Relationship?” Ariadne finished for him.

Hal looked over at her, squinting thoughtfully.  “Right.  I guess that’s it.  We co-exist.  Sort of like—“  Embarrassed that he’d said too much, Hal cleared his throat and looked down at the floor again.

Elliott was the first to say what they were thinking.  “Sort of like what?”

The other two, wanting to chastise him, but glad he had asked, turned their reddening faces downward, avoiding eye contact.

Hal stared at Elliott for a moment, seemingly analyzing again how much to say.  Finally, he threw one hand in the air in frustration.  “I don’t know what I’m worried about,” he said a little too loud.  “It’s not like you know my family or that we’ll ever see each other again.  Or maybe it’s that we’ll never see them again.  I don’t know.”  He seemed on the verge of anger.

“Sort of like me and my family, Elliott.  We co-exist.  I’ve been sitting here listening to Candace talk about that young family and as poor as they are and as hard as they have it, you know what? I envy them!  If any one of them was here, the others would be going crazy.  They would be missed.  My family would miss the idea of me more than the real me.  Even my wife.  We live in the same house, but we are not together.  I don’t know how much longer we can keep up the façade.”  He chuckled and shook his head.  “Guess now we don’t have to worry about that either.”

After a long silence, it was Candace who spoke.  “So you’re angry with God about all that?”

Hal looked quickly up at her.  He opened and closed his mouth twice before he finally spoke, his face going from surprised to angry to a type of calm acceptance.  Nodding, he said, “Yeah, I guess I am.  Nothing in my life is working out the way I thought it was supposed to.  I’ve tried to be obedient to what He wanted, tried to be led, but where has it gotten me?  A job I hate, two kids who ignore me, and a wife who doesn’t know me anymore.”

“Wow….” Elliott said after a pause.  “I had you pegged as the level-headed one in this group.”

The three stared at him for a moment, then burst into simultaneous, albeit nervous, laughter.  Even Hal chuckled.  Elliott looked confused, but relieved.

“Oh don’t worry,” Hal said.  “I’m still pretty good about doing the ‘right thing.’  I just sometimes wish my heart could catch up with my brain.”

Ariadne reached across the table between them and patted his hand.  “Maybe that’s why you’re here,” she said, smiling kindly at him.  

Hal looked thoughtful, picked up his coffee cup and took the last cold swallow.  “Maybe,” he said quietly, then quickly added, “I need some more coffee.”

The other three stood with him and led the way into the kitchen with their empty plates.  Hearing a small crash, they turned, then gasped.

The empty coffee cup was shattered on the floor.  

Hal was gone.

Chapter 10

When Phil Nixon heard that his big brother and his sister-in-law planned on naming their baby boy Harold, he was royally ticked off.

“He’s gonna get beat up,” he complained.

After a lengthy argument about the family history of the name, the meaning behind it (they swore it meant strong and mighty, but he looked it up in a baby name book and it meant “army leader”), Phil finally begged them to at least call the little fellow Hal.  The parents-to-be shrugged, Phil took that as agreement, and never called his nephew anything but Hal.

Little Hal was his Uncle Phil’s shadow.  A dad busy with a white collar job, and a mom who followed up on her baby-delivering success by having twin girls fourteen months later, little Hal adored the attention by his uncle and was happy to be his constant tagalong.  It was Uncle Phil who taught him to throw a ball, ride a bike, and later to target shoot with arrows and, much later, guns (he was sworn to secrecy about that one).  Uncle Phil took him to ball games, taught him that real men love music, made sure he had cool t-shirts and ball caps to wear.  Uncle Phil taught him to spit, cuss, and, when he was too young to know he was doing it for the benefit of his Uncle Phil, bat his eyes at cute women.  

The one thing Uncle Phil didn’t teach him was how to swim.  Big, tough, larger-than-life Uncle Phil had one Achilles heel – he was mortally afraid of water.  A near-drowning incident as a very young child could never be entirely overcome.  Although he tried never to let his nephew see his fear, it didn’t take a genius to figure it out.  Once, on a trip to San Antonio to see the Alamo, the two walked along the Riverwalk.  Hal was fascinated and walked on the edge, looking down into the water.  Phil, as close to the shops or walls as he could get, could finally stand it no more and snatched his nephew suddenly away, mounted the next set of steps, and went up to street level.  He had to sit down on a bench to get his breathing back to normal.  Hal was six and the fear registered.  His boy’s mind reasoned that if Uncle Phil was afraid of it, it was truly a force to be feared.  From that point on, Hal, too, avoided water.

And now here he sat.  In a boat, alone, nothing but water all around him.

Hal gripped the edges of the small craft and looked around.  He was in just a small boat, three plank seats going from side to side.  He was on the back seat.  Behind him was a motor.  He recognized it as the type you crank up like a lawn mower, then use the protruding stick to steer.

Steer where?  Hal squinted into the sun and looked around, never letting go of the sides of the boat.  There didn’t appear to be land anywhere.  Hal felt his heart beginning to race, his body going cold, then immediately hot.  Darkening clouds slid over the sun and gave him some relief from the glare and the heat.  May in Texas is August elsewhere.  If, indeed, that’s when it was.  And where he was.

Hal felt his stomach rumble and willed himself to calm down.  He looked up and cocked his head to one side.

“Water?” he said out loud.  “Really?”

Frowning, Hal shook his head.  Elliott got music, Candace got a story.  What does he get?  His number one fear.  Lovely.

The bobbing of the boat in the slightly choppy water threatened both to unnerve Hal even more and put his nervous stomach right over the edge.  Forcing himself to watch the water, he realized it appeared to be moving against him, meaning, if his non-nautical sense was correct, land was likely behind him.  Good enough for me, he thought, and turned to the motor to try to figure out how to use it.  It had a pull cord, a lot like a lawn mower.  Hal pulled.  Nothing happened.  He pulled harder.  On the third try the motor cranked.  The boat, however, didn’t move.

Hal frowned.  Using his engineer’s brain, he examined the motor.  There was a knob on the side by him, like a lawn mower choke.  Looking upward again and shaking his head, acknowledging the on-going cosmic joke, Hal pulled the knob all the way out.  The boat lurched forward.

Lurching was not something Hal was prepared for.  He bounced off the plank seat, the boat twisting in the water as Hal’s hand was ripped from the rudder stick.  Crying out, Hal desperately grabbed the sides of the boat again, pulling himself back onto the seat and willing the little craft not to flip over.  Grabbing the stick to try to steady the boat, Hal at first overcompensated, creating a side-to-side flipping that threatened to finally either send him flying over the side of the boat or cause him to lose his omelet over the side of the boat.  Finally, the boat steadied, but it was going too fast and Hal had no idea which direction he was heading.  Thinking he just had to stop the thing and get his bearings again, but not wanting to let go of the rudder stick, he forced his left hand off the side of the boat and reached over to push in the knob in.  He only got it part way in when he had to grab the side again to balance himself.  The boat slowed down.

Ah, Hal thought.  So you’re the gas pedal.  Right.  

Leaving it at that speed, Hal looked around again.  Had he turned around?  What direction was he going?  Did it matter, really?  Hal squinted into the distance and looked as far to his left as he could without turning around or letting go of the stick.  Nothing.  He looked forward again, then moved his head slowly to the right.  He considered pushing the knob fully back in.  It seemed foolish to continue moving and not know where he was heading.  But then, off to the right, about two o’clock, he could see something.  Hal frowned.  It was too small to even tell what it was, but it was something.  

Only proverbial fish in this sea, Hal reasoned, moving the rudder stick just enough to head him in the direction of whatever it was that had broken the horizon line.  Anxious to get to anything that might get him out of this water, Hal pulled the knob back out a bit to speed up the boat.  Trying not to think about all the what-ifs, trying not to think of how very much water was around him, and certainly trying not to think of how very much water was below him, Hal focused on the mystery spot and pulled the button out a little bit more.

 It was another boat.  Hal groaned when he realized it, but he was committed now and felt like continuing toward it.  It was a nice boat, a cabin cruiser.  Not a yacht, but pretty nice sized.  As Hal got closer, slowing his little boat down, he could see movement on the deck.  Someone was standing there.  But not just standing – whoever it was was waving their arms wildly, a jacket or something in their hands, waving side to side like a flag.  Frowning, Hal pulled the knob out a bit to speed up.  It was a lady and she seemed frantic.

As Hal got closer, he could hear her yelling.  “Help!  Please stop!”

Wondering briefly where she thought he was going, Hal waved back to encourage her, then awkwardly steered his little craft around to the back of her larger one where he saw there was a platform where he could get onto her boat.  His heart catching at the thought of the transition from boat to boat, Hal pushed the button in, killing the engine.  He looked over helplessly as he drifted past the boat, but didn’t have to worry long.  A rope landed in front of him.

“Grab it!” It was the woman, holding her end of the rope.  

Hal obediently grabbed the rope.  The woman brought up the slack in the line, then pulled, Hal and his little craft sliding backward until the side of his boat was right up against the back of the woman’s.  Hal saw she was tying the rope off to a ring on the back of her boat.  Praying quickly, he stood, the boat rocking, his arms shooting out to the side to balance him.  The woman was there, leaning toward him, holding out her hand.  Her face was strained, pinched with worry and fear.

“Oh please hurry,” she said.  It was more of a prayer than a reprimand.

Worried now, Hal took her hand and stepped from the small craft onto the larger one.  Suddenly he found himself being embraced by the woman who began sobbing into his chest.  His eyebrows up, Hal slowly brought his arms around her, but not touching her, patting her reassuringly with the tips of his fingers.

“What’s-?” But that’s as far as he got.  The woman suddenly straightened, wiping her eyes and shaking her head.

“Oh thank God you’re here!  Please, hurry.  My husband…. Do you have a cell phone?”  Her face was frantic.

Hal found himself patting his pockets.  “Uh, no – “

Then she was off like a shot, a confused Hal hurrying to keep up with her.  

They went through the open back of the boat, and down some steps into a darker area.  Hal could see through open doors a kitchen area, a bathroom, and at least two bedrooms.  He couldn’t help but thinking if he could park this thing on concrete, he wouldn’t mind living in it.  The woman went through the open door straight ahead – another bedroom.  Only this one was occupied.  

A man lay on the bed.  He was on his back, his right leg raised up on a platform of pillows.  There was a towel or something wrapped around his lower leg and it was soaked through with blood.  The woman was up at the man’s head, brushing back his hair, picking up a nearby cloth and wiping his face.  Hal could hear her talking quietly.  As he approached, he heard, “Someone’s here.  It’s a miracle.  You’ll be okay now, you’ll see.”

Hal stopped and took in the picture as quickly as possible.  The man was probably forty.  He appeared to be conscious, but just barely.  His hair was wet.  He was wearing what appeared to be swimming trunks – the old fashioned, non-embarrassing kind, Hal noticed.  He had a short-sleeved shirt on, unbuttoned to his navel.  He was frowning, his eyes closed and his head lolling a bit on the pillow.  He appeared to be trying to speak.

The woman shushed him, looking frantically at Hal.  Funny he hadn’t noticed before, but she was gorgeous.  She couldn’t have been more than thirty, if that.  Blonde hair that she was given at birth and not at the hairdresser.  She was wearing a bikini with a sheer cover over it.  She had model-perfect skin and body.  Hal suddenly realized she looked very familiar.

“Please help him,” she said, her voice strained but not panicked.  

Startled into movement, Hal moved forward toward the bed.  “What happened?”

The woman continued to wipe the man’s face with the cloth.  “The boat quit.  There’s a something-or-other that keeps messing up in the motor.  Kenny knows all about it, so he jumped in to fix it.  Then as he was swimming away, it came on all of a sudden.  We had forgotten to turn it off up here.  His leg got pulled in and the propeller blade tore it up.”  Her voice caught and she looked over at Hal, new tears on her face.  “Do something.” She pleaded.  “Get us out of here.  He needs a doctor.”  The tears were flowing freely now.

Hal saw the man’s right arm slowly come up.  He took the woman’s arm in his hand.  She gasped and grabbed his hand in both of hers.

“Baby! Kenny!  Are you okay?”

The man’s eyes were open.  Hal could see the pain was great.  His voice raspy and barely audible, the man made an attempt at a smile and said, “Bibs, can you get me some more water?”

The woman straightened quickly.  “Of course!”  Grabbing the empty glass on the table next to the bed, she turned and practically fled past Hal.  When he looked back at the man on the bed, Hal saw he was being watched.  Scrutinized was actually more like it.  The man made a small movement calling Hal toward him.  When he got to the side of the bed, Hal was surprised at the amount of suspicion and almost animosity in the man’s face.  It was the oddest mix of fatigue, pain, and anger.

“Who are you?” the man demanded.

“Name’s Hal Nixon,” Hal answered.

“Where did you come from?”

Without thinking, Hal answered quickly, “I have no idea.”

The man frowned at him.  Winced.  Closed his eyes, apparently fighting a wave of pain.

The sounds of the approaching woman could be heard.  Suddenly, the man grabbed Hal’s arm with surprising strength.

“Can I trust you?” he said, his eyes probing desperately.

A wave of compassion rolled over Hal.  He laid his hand on top of the man’s and leaned over the bed.  Looking the man in the eye, hoping his sincerity was obvious, Hal said, “Yes, sir.  I’ll do whatever I can to help.”

The man watched him for a moment, then Hal felt his entire body relax as he sighed, his hand dropping back onto the bed, his eyes closing again.

The woman rushed up with the glass of water, pushing in front of Hal and sliding her hand under the man’s head to help lift it. After a couple sips of water, she laid his head back on the pillow, replaced the glass on the table, wiped his face with the cloth again.  She glanced sideways at Hal.

“What are we going to do?”

He was afraid she was going to say that.

“You’ve radioed for help?”

The woman’s face became even more tight with worry.  Shaking her head, she said, “Nothing works.  We’re dead in the water.  The stupid motor quit again.  I can’t get a radio signal.  My cell phone doesn’t even work.”  The whole time she talked, she kept wiping her husband’s face with the cloth.  Her movements were getting faster, jerkier.  Hal began to worry that she was about to completely fall apart.  

Swallowing hard, Hal took a step toward her and laid a gentle hand on her arm.  When she looked at him, his heart caught.  She was so frightened and looked so vulnerable.  Hal smiled, hoping it looked genuine and comforting.  “We’ll figure something out,” he said softly.  “What’s your name?”

She looked surprised at the question.  “I’m Bibby Coonts,” she said as if he should know that.  Hal frowned and looked at her a little sideways.  Bibby Coonts.  Not your everyday name…. Suddenly Hal’s eyes widened and the woman smiled for the first time.  Nodding, she said, “I know.  I look a little different up close, and with no makeup on.”

Hal’s mouth had dropped open.  He knew he was staring, but couldn’t stop.  Bibby Coonts.  Just about the best contemporary blues singer in the world.  Hal had all her cds, but had never seen her in concert.  The photo on the cover of the disc didn’t do her justice.  She lent a sultry, haunting quality to some of the slower, traditional standards, yet could transform her voice into a lilting, uplifting near-soprano for some of the faster, more upbeat numbers.  Not a big fan of contemporary anything, even Hal liked her original music, much of it written by or co-written with someone named Kenny Schwartz.  Hal’s eyes widened again and he looked at the man on the bed.  She’d called him Kenny and referred to him as her husband.

Reading his thoughts, Bibby nodded again.  “Yes, this is Kenny Schwartz.  He’s my partner in every sense of the word.  We have a house in Dallas, but spend most of our time out here on the lake.  This has never happened before – losing all our power.”  She sighed, the frown deepening again as she glanced over at her sleeping or unconscious husband.  Looking up at Hal, she suddenly tipped her head at him.  “Where did you come from?” she asked, unknowingly echoing her husband’s question.

Hal shook his head and waved his hand a little, as if to deflect the question.  “My name is Hal,” he said, suddenly feeling like a schoolboy.  “Hal Nixon.  I, uh, I have to say that I’m, well, I really….” He wanted to kick himself.  “That is, I truly enjoy your music, Ms. Coonts.”

Bibby grinned.  “Bibby, please.  And thank you.  I don’t know how you got here, but you are like a miracle.  I’m so worried for Kenny.  He’s lost so much blood.”

Snapped out of his idol-worshipping reverie, Hal turned to Kenny.  The towel was soaked through with blood, the outer edges of it turning dark.  He’d had it on his leg for awhile.  “Do you have anything else we can put on his leg?  A clean sheet maybe?”

Bibby’s eyes widened and she ran again from the room.  Hal took the opportunity to carefully peel the towel wrapping away from Kenny’s leg.  The man stirred somewhat as the covering moved and Hal nearly wretched when he saw the damage done by the boat’s propeller.  It looked more like raw meat than a leg and now that it was unwrapped, Hal could see it was slightly twisted at an unnatural angle, the bones obviously broken, if not crushed.  Panic began to build with the bile in his stomach as he stared at the leg.  Frozen by revulsion and ignorance, he just stood there, until he felt a hand grip his arm, causing him to startle.

Kenny Schwartz was looking at him, his face pale, his eyes probing.  “She’s not so tough,” he said, the effort to speak obvious.  Hal recognized the man’s need to get it in before she returned.  “Get her out of here.  Bring help back.  Just…” His body jerked suddenly and he grimaced, his eyes closing.  Gasping slightly, he looked at Hal, his eyes intense.  “Get her out of here.”

Hal frowned, looked back at the gruesome leg, then up at the eyes of the husband.

Trying to sound more sure than he felt, he firmly gripped Kenny’s hand.  “We’re getting you both out of here.”

Chapter 11

Candace put down the book she was reading and looked up at Ariadne.  They were sitting in the spaces that seemed designated for them – Candace on the right end of the couch, Ariadne in the wing-back chair diagonal from her.  Ariadne had pulled an ottoman up in front of her chair and had her feet resting on it, crossed at the ankles.  She was crocheting, a finished piece of beautiful summer colors almost miraculously appearing and spreading out over her small outstretched legs.  Candace cocked her head and watched the creator and the creation.  It was amazing how quickly and with apparent effortlessness the piece had materialized.  

“Have you been doing that for awhile?” Candace asked.

Glancing up briefly, then back down at her hands, Ariadne said, “Several hours.”

Candace grinned, then cleared her throat.  “No,” she said gently and with no condescension, “I mean crocheting in general, not that piece specifically.”

“What’s happ’ning?” he barked at her.  

Ebony lifted one eyebrow and tipped her head at him.  “Watch your tone, young man.  I don’t have any idea what’s happening.  I was just on the subway –“

“Me too!” both men answered at once.

Ebony frowned at them.  “I thought there was an accident,” she said.

“There was!”

Ebony looked around her at the grass and trees.  “Then,” she said, her voice getting quieter, “where are we?”

The tall man just shrugged and looked like he was about to cry.

“Hey!”  It was the young man, pointing.  “Maybe we can find some help there!”

Ebony and the tall man looked in the direction he was pointing.  Ahead of them they could see a house.  A yellow, country-looking house.  There was nothing else around them as far as the eye could see.

Ebony sighed and lifted her shoulders.  “Well,” she said, “I don’t see no other choice.  Let’s go see if anybody’s home.”

The three walked through the grass and flowers toward the yellow house, hoping it held some answers for them.
